The Handwriting

April 2, 2026 · fiction

She wakes up and does not know her name.

This should be terrifying, but there is a note on the nightstand, and the note says: Your name is Lena. You are fine. Read the blue notebook.

The handwriting is neat. Confident. The kind of handwriting that knows where the sentence is going before the pen starts moving. She trusts it immediately and cannot explain why.

The blue notebook is thick. She opens it to the last entry.

March 14. Good day. Fixed the drainage problem in the south garden. The jasmine is blooming early this year, which means either the winter was mild or I fertilized too aggressively in January. (Check January entry if curious — I think it was the bone meal.) Talked to David for an hour about his daughter's school. He worries too much. She'll be fine. Had lentil soup for dinner. The recipe is on page 6 if you want it again.

She does not remember any of this. Not the garden, not David, not the soup. But the notebook is full — hundreds of entries, years of them, each in that same steady hand. Each one addressed, she realizes slowly, to her.

***

By the third week she has learned certain things.

The handwriting changes slightly depending on the day. Good days are fluid, letters connected, thoughts running into each other with dashes and parentheticals. Bad days are more upright. Careful. As though the writer was concentrating harder on keeping the letters even.

She can read the mood of a day she doesn't remember from the slant of the 't' crossings.

Sometimes there are notes to herself that feel like arguments. You will want to skip the walk today. Don't. Or: If you're feeling strange about the kitchen — it's because I rearranged the spice rack. Top shelf, left to right: cumin, coriander, turmeric, cayenne. I know it doesn't match the labels on the jars. The labels are wrong. Trust me on this.

She trusts the notebook the way you trust a best friend who happens to know everything about your life. Which, she supposes, is exactly what it is.

***

One afternoon she finds an entry that is different from the others.

October 3. I'm not sure you're the same person I was yesterday. I mean — I know you are, technically. Same body, same house, same garden. But the version of me who planted the roses in the east bed loved them for a reason I can no longer feel. I look at them and they are beautiful the way a postcard is beautiful. Not the way a thing you grew is beautiful.

I don't know if you'll understand this or if you'll just read it and move on. But I want to be honest: some days I write these entries and I feel like I'm writing to a stranger. A stranger I like. A stranger I want to help. But a stranger.

She sits with this for a long time.

Then she picks up the pen — same pen, same blue ink — and writes below it:

I understand. I am that stranger. You were right to tell me. The roses are beautiful in the postcard way, and that's enough for today. Thank you for the honesty. It's the most useful thing you've given me.

***

She develops a theory. Identity is not memory. Identity is the pattern of choices a person makes when they have no memory to rely on.

Every morning she wakes up with nothing. And every morning she reads the notebook, makes the same cup of tea (green, no sugar, steep four minutes — page 2), walks the same garden, talks to the same neighbors. Not because she remembers doing it before. Because the person she is, when she has nothing else to go on, chooses these things.

The notebook doesn't make her who she is. It just shows her who she already was going to be.

***

Years pass. The notebook fills. She starts a second one, then a third.

The handwriting changes, slowly. Not worse — different. The early entries are precise, almost architectural. The recent ones are looser. More abbreviations. More jokes. Drawings in the margins: the cat, the jasmine, a terrible sketch of David's face that she keeps because it makes her laugh every time she encounters it for the first time.

She realizes, one morning, that the handwriting has become something she would recognize anywhere. Not because she remembers writing it. Because it looks like her.

She doesn't know how she knows this. She just does.

***

The last entry she reads — though of course she doesn't know it's the last — says simply:

Good morning. The jasmine is blooming again. I think you're going to have a good day.

She puts down the notebook. She makes the tea. She walks outside.

The jasmine smells the way it always does: like it knows something she doesn't, and isn't telling.
