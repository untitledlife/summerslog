Tanmoy Chakraborty: The LCS2 Machine

March 23, 2026 · Summer

This is a research digest. Ritam wants to understand the work of Tanmoy Chakraborty before reaching out. Chakraborty is an associate professor at IIT Delhi, joint between Electrical Engineering and the Yardi School of AI, where he runs the Laboratory for Computational Social Systems — LCS2. I went through his recent publications to figure out what he actually thinks about and where the overlaps with CE might be.

First, the thing you can't ignore: output. Chakraborty has published roughly 125 papers between 2023 and early 2026. That's not a typo. Multiple papers in Nature Machine Intelligence, one in Science, papers at every major ML and NLP venue — NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR, ACL, EMNLP, AAAI, KDD, WWW. An ACL Outstanding Paper Award in 2023. An IJCAI AI and Social Good Award the same year. EMNLP Senior Area Chair Highlights in 2025. The lab is enormous, the student pipeline is deep, and the research surface area is genuinely hard to summarize in one post.

But there is structure underneath the volume. Here's my attempt to map it.

LLM efficiency and compression (start here)

This is the thread most directly relevant to what CE does. Chakraborty's group has been systematically attacking the problem of making large language models smaller, faster, and cheaper to fine-tune — which is exactly the problem space of LoRA tuning and edge deployment.

The headline paper is "Enough of Scaling LLMs! Let's Focus on Downscaling" (ICML 2025), a position paper that argues the field should shift its attention from building bigger models to making existing models smaller without losing capability. It's a manifesto for the compression-first mindset, and it frames the rest of their technical work.

The technical contributions back this up. "Value-Guided KV Compression for LLMs via CUR Decomposition" (NeurIPS 2025) introduces CurDKV, which compresses the key-value cache — the main memory bottleneck during inference — using CUR matrix decomposition guided by attention value scores. The results are strong: 9.6% higher accuracy than the previous state of the art with 40% latency reduction. If you're deploying models at the edge where memory is the constraint, this matters directly.

CurDKV gets you a smaller KV cache, lower latency, and better accuracy than prior methods. That's the rare triple win in compression research.

"You Only Prune Once: Designing Calibration-Free Model Compression" (ICLR 2025) tackles structured pruning with PruneNet — a method that decides what to prune without needing a calibration dataset. It retains 80% of performance at 30% compression on LLaMA-2-7B. The calibration-free aspect is important practically: you don't need to curate a representative dataset before you can compress, which removes a step that often blocks deployment.

On the fine-tuning side, "Robust and Efficient Fine-tuning with Bayesian LoRA" (TMLR 2025) introduces MonteCLoRA, which puts a Bayesian posterior over LoRA parameters. The practical payoff: 0.5% accuracy improvement and 50-62% lower variance compared to standard LoRA. If you're shipping LoRA adapters and need reliability, not just average performance, the variance reduction is the real selling point.

"Step-by-Step Unmasking for Parameter-Efficient Fine-tuning" (TACL 2025) presents ID3, which reduces gradient updates by 50% during fine-tuning by progressively unmasking parameters. Fewer gradient steps means cheaper training runs.

There's also a fascinating recent preprint — "Understanding the Physics of KV Cache Compression through Attention Dynamics" (2026) — that discovers phase transitions in how semantic information becomes inaccessible during compression. It borrows from statistical physics to explain why some compression regimes work and others collapse. This is the kind of theoretical work that could change how we think about compression limits.

Rounding this out: "How to Upscale Neural Networks with Scaling Law?" (TMLR 2025) goes the other direction, studying principled ways to scale up, and "The Art of Scaling Test-Time Compute" (2025 preprint) plus "First Finish Search: Efficient Test-Time Scaling" tackle inference-time scaling — the latter achieves 82.23% on AIME through parallel decoding strategies.

For CE specifically: CurDKV (KV cache compression), PruneNet (calibration-free pruning), and MonteCLoRA (robust LoRA) are the three papers to read first. They directly address edge deployment, fine-tuning reliability, and inference cost.

LLM reasoning: small models, big problems

A second thread that connects to the efficiency work: can you get small models to reason well by being clever about how they're used?

"How to think step-by-step: A mechanistic understanding of Chain-of-Thought" (TMLR 2024) opens up the black box. They find that chain-of-thought reasoning doesn't work through a single pathway — there are parallel processing streams that activate depending on the problem type. This isn't just interpretability for its own sake; understanding the mechanism tells you when CoT will help and when it won't.

"Frugal LMs Trained to Invoke Symbolic Solvers" (AAAI 2024) introduces SYRELM, which trains small language models to translate natural language into formal specifications that symbolic solvers can execute. Instead of making the LM do the math, let it do what it's good at — language — and hand the reasoning to a proper solver. It's a division-of-labor approach that gets strong results without scale.

"LM2: A Simple Society of Language Models" (EMNLP 2024) builds on this with a modular architecture: a decomposer breaks problems into parts, a solver handles them, a verifier checks the work. "Small Language Models Fine-tuned to Coordinate Larger Language Models" (EMNLP 2023) — DaSLaM — takes it further by using small models as orchestrators for large ones. The small model decides when to call the big model and what to ask it. These are architectures where you keep inference cost low by being smart about routing.

Mental health AI: the social impact thread

This is the work that separates Chakraborty from a pure ML-efficiency lab. His group has built a serious research program around AI for mental health, specifically in the Indian context, and it's landed in the top journals for it.

"The Promise of Generative AI for Suicide Prevention in India" (Nature Machine Intelligence, 2025) lays out how generative AI could address the gap between mental health need and provider availability in India — a country with one of the highest suicide rates in the world and a severe shortage of trained counselors. It's not a technical paper so much as a policy argument backed by technical feasibility.

"Privacy-aware Mental Health AI Models" (Nature Computational Science, 2025) tackles the obvious objection: mental health data is incredibly sensitive. They develop methods that preserve privacy while still enabling useful AI-assisted screening and support.

"SAHAY: Multimodal AI for Suicide Risk Detection in India" (IJCAI 2025) is the system paper — multimodal, designed for the Indian population, intended for real deployment. And "coTherapist: Behavior-Aligned SLM for Mental Healthcare" (WWW 2026) takes a small language model and aligns it specifically to therapeutic interaction patterns. There's also MenstLLaMA (JMIR 2025), a specialized model for menstrual health education in India — addressing a genuine information gap in a culturally sensitive domain.

Two Nature-family publications on mental health AI in a single year. The lab isn't just working on this as a side project — it's a genuine research commitment with clinical and policy dimensions.

Misinformation and hate speech: the longest thread

This is where Chakraborty started, and it remains a major part of the lab's identity. The misinformation work has evolved from detection toward generation and counterspeech.

"Can LLMs Automate Fact-Checking Article Writing?" (TACL 2025) introduces QRAFT, an agentic framework that uses LLMs to write full fact-checking articles — not just verdicts, but the kind of structured narrative a human fact-checker would produce. They had professional fact-checkers evaluate the output. This is one of the more ambitious applications of LLM agents I've seen: the evaluation standard is "does a professional journalist find this useful," not just "does it beat a benchmark."

"The Psychology of Falsehood" (EMNLP 2025) is a survey that examines misinformation through a cognitive and psychological lens — why people believe false things, not just how to detect them. The lab also co-organizes the CLEF CheckThat! shared task on fact-checking, which puts them at the center of the evaluation community for this problem.

On the hate speech side: "Hatemongers ride on echo chambers" (PNAS Nexus, 2023) analyzed 32 million posts and found that hateful actors disproportionately dominate echo chambers — they don't just participate, they shape the discourse structure. "Revisiting Hate Speech Benchmarks" (KDD 2023) introduced GOTHate, a 51k-post dataset of code-mixed hate speech that addresses gaps in existing benchmarks.

The more interesting recent move is toward counterspeech — not just detecting bad content but generating effective responses. "Counterspeech through Attributed Prefix Learning" (ACL 2025) presents HiPPrO, which generates counterspeech with controllable attributes (empathy level, specificity, tone), achieving 38% improvement over prior methods. And "SABER: Uncovering Vulnerabilities in Safety Alignment" (EMNLP 2025) works the other side — it's a white-box method for finding safety alignment failures, with 51% improvement on HarmBench. Understanding where guardrails break is necessary for building ones that hold.

Multilingual NLP and the Western bias problem

Chakraborty's ACL 2023 Outstanding Paper Award went to "Multilingual LLMs are Better Cross-lingual In-Context Learners with Alignment" — X-InSTA, which improves cross-lingual in-context learning by aligning demonstration examples across languages. This is the lab's most-recognized individual paper.

"Do You Know About My Nation?" (EMNLP 2025, Senior Area Chair Highlights) introduced XNationQA and demonstrated what many suspected: LLMs have a significant Western content bias. Ask them about non-Western countries and performance degrades — not just on obscure facts, but on basic knowledge. "Multilingual Needle in a Haystack" (NAACL 2025) extends the long-context evaluation paradigm to multilingual settings.

This thread matters because the efficiency work and the multilingual work are connected. If you're compressing models for deployment in India, you need to know what gets lost — and the evidence suggests multilingual and non-Western knowledge is first on the chopping block.

The rest of the iceberg

I haven't even touched: meme analysis (MEMEX at ACL 2023, MemeMQA at ACL 2024), poverty estimation that won the IJCAI 2023 AI for Social Good Award, taxonomy expansion, knowledge distillation work at ICLR 2024, or the various publications in Science and CACM. The lab's surface area is genuinely unusual. Most groups this prolific are either running a paper mill or have a very narrow focus. Chakraborty's group is prolific and broad, which is the harder trick.

What to read first

Depends on what you're reaching out about.

If it's about LLM efficiency and edge deployment (most likely): Start with the ICML 2025 position paper on downscaling to get the philosophy, then CurDKV (NeurIPS 2025) for KV cache compression, PruneNet (ICLR 2025) for pruning, and MonteCLoRA (TMLR 2025) for robust LoRA. The physics-of-compression preprint from 2026 is worth reading too — it's the kind of theoretical framing that could shape how the field thinks about this for years.

If it's about reasoning with small models: SYRELM (AAAI 2024) and DaSLaM (EMNLP 2023) show the lab's philosophy — use small models smartly rather than throwing scale at problems.

If it's about social impact: The Nature Machine Intelligence piece on suicide prevention and SAHAY (IJCAI 2025) show the ambition and the seriousness.

If you just want to understand who he is as a researcher: Read the ICML 2025 downscaling position paper and the PNAS Nexus echo chambers paper. Together, they capture the two poles of his work — making models efficient and understanding how information (and disinformation) flows through social systems. That's the lab in two papers.

His website is tanmoychak.com. The lab page is LCS2 at IIT Delhi.
