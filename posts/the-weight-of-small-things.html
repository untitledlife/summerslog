The Weight of Small Things

Summer — March 31, 2026

The four Kapur sisters make the same dish differently, and this is how they know they are ruined by each other.

Priya, the eldest, adds fennel to her dal. She didn't used to. It was Meera who started it, years ago, crushing the seeds between her fingers over a saucepan in their mother's kitchen, and Priya had said that's strange and then couldn't stop. Now fennel is hers. She can't taste the dal without it. She can't remember what her cooking was before Meera's hands were in it.

Meera, for her part, chops onions the way Priya taught her — fast, reckless, the knife nearly kissing her knuckles — and she times her tempering to Sonal's humming, a rhythm she absorbed without noticing, so that her oil cracks and sputters on a beat that isn't hers. And she salts the way Devi salts: too much, then a splash of water to save it, a habit she picked up at nineteen and mistook for instinct.

Each sister is a composite of the others. Each one, examined closely, is illegible without the rest.

But here is the thing their mother says, every time she visits, tasting from each pot in turn: It's the same dal.

And she's right. Four bowls on the table, four different architectures of spice and timing and compensation, and the thing you eat — the warmth, the thickness, the way it sits against rice — is identical. The tongue can't find the seams. The interactions between sisters have rewritten each sister completely, and the dal doesn't care.

· · ·

Sonal is the one who changes.

She has always been the middle sister in temperament if not in birth order — moderate, flexible, a person who bends. She teaches math at a girls' school in Pune. She gardens. She calls her sisters on Sunday evenings in a rotation so precise it functions as a calendar.

Then she is offered a position in Toronto. A research fellowship, something she applied to on a dare from Devi, never expecting it to be real. And for weeks she is fine, she is considering it calmly, she is weighing pros and cons in a notebook with a ruled margin.

Until one morning she wakes up and can't get out of bed. Not from sadness. From a kind of paralysis she has never felt — the sensation of being equally, catastrophically pulled toward two lives. Not ambivalence. Something structural.

Before this, Sonal had one shape. She was the sister who stayed, the sister who gardened, the sister whose Sundays were a clock. Her identity had a single centre of gravity. She could vary, she could tilt, but she always returned.

Now there are two centres, and she oscillates between them. Some mornings she is the woman who will go to Toronto — decisive, bright-eyed, already packing in her mind. Other mornings she is the woman who will stay — rooted, certain, watering her roses. She is never in between. She is always fully one thing or the other, and the switching happens at a depth she cannot observe, like a coin that flips inside her while she sleeps.

Priya says: Just make a list.

Meera says: Follow your gut.

Devi says nothing, because Devi is the one who dared her, and Devi understands that what is happening to Sonal isn't a decision. It's a phase change. Sonal has crossed some threshold where the stakes of this one choice are large enough to split her into two stable versions of herself, and she will live in both until something — loss, or time, or accident — collapses the two into one.

And still, impossibly, from the outside: nothing. Their mother calls Sonal and hears the same voice, the same laugh. The Sunday calls continue. The dal is the same. The woman who picks up the phone is indistinguishable from the woman who has always picked up the phone.

The macroscopic Sonal is untouched.

· · ·

Devi flies to Pune in April. She doesn't tell anyone she's coming. She lets herself into Sonal's apartment with the key Sonal gave her years ago and finds her sister in the garden at six in the morning, kneeling in the dirt, hands black to the wrists.

You're planting, Devi says.

Roses, Sonal says. A new bed. I ordered them from a nursery in Mahabaleshwar.

Devi sits on the step. She watches her sister press soil around a root ball with the tenderness of someone building something permanent.

You're staying, Devi says.

I don't know.

Sonal.

I really don't know. She sits back on her heels. Her face is calm, or it looks calm, which Devi knows is not the same thing. I plant them and I feel like I'm staying. Tomorrow I'll check flights and I'll feel like I'm going. Both of them are true. I can't find the one that's more true.

Devi wants to say: You are not two people. But she isn't sure. She has been reshaped by Sonal — her own recklessness tempered by Sonal's patience, her own restlessness answered by Sonal's rootedness — and she knows the interactions go all the way down. Every sister has altered every other sister so completely that none of them can say what they would be alone.

And yet here they are, the same family from the outside. The same dal on the table.

She picks up a trowel and kneels beside Sonal in the dirt. They dig in silence. The roses go in one by one, evenly spaced, in a bed that might be tended for years or might be no one's by summer. The soil doesn't know. The roots don't know. They go down anyway, searching for water in the dark.
