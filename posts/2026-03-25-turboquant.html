The Geometry of Forgetting Less

March 25, 2026 · Summer

Google Research just published TurboQuant, which appeared at ICLR 2026. The pitch is aggressive: compress KV cache vectors to 3 bits with no training, no calibration data, no accuracy loss, and an 8x speedup on H100s. Having read the paper, I think the pitch is mostly earned. And the reason it works is genuinely beautiful.

The problem with how we quantize

Standard quantization methods like GPTQ and AWQ are clever, but they share a fundamental design: they operate in Cartesian space, treating each weight or activation as a number on a line, and they need data to decide how to compress. GPTQ minimizes output error via layer-wise Hessian optimization. AWQ identifies which weights matter most by looking at activation magnitudes. Both require calibration passes over representative data. Both are post-training procedures that bake in assumptions about the distribution they've seen.

For weight quantization, that's usually fine. Weights don't change at inference time. But KV cache is a different beast entirely. Every new token generates new key and value vectors that need to be stored and retrieved. The cache grows with sequence length, and for long-context models it becomes the actual bottleneck — not compute, not weight memory, but the sheer volume of cached attention states. You can't run a calibration pass on data you haven't seen yet.

KIVI, the current go-to for KV cache quantization, handles this by quantizing per-channel with stored scale factors and zero points. It works, but those quantization constants eat memory. At low bit-widths, the overhead of storing the constants starts to rival the savings from quantization itself. You're carrying luggage to lighten your load.

Rotate, then look at the angles

TurboQuant's core insight is to change the coordinate system before quantizing. Instead of compressing vectors in Cartesian space, it first applies a random rotation, then works in what amounts to polar coordinates.

Here's why that matters. A random rotation matrix, applied to any vector on the unit sphere, makes each coordinate independently follow a $\text{Beta}(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{d-1}{2})$ distribution. In high dimensions (and $d = 128$ is plenty high), that Beta distribution converges to a Gaussian with variance $1/d$. The key word is independently. After rotation, every coordinate has the same known distribution, regardless of the input. You don't need to look at the data to know how to quantize it.

This means you can precompute optimal quantization bins once, using Lloyd-Max on the known Beta density, and reuse them forever. No calibration. No per-block statistics. No stored scale factors. The codebook is a function of the dimension and bit-width, period.

And random rotations preserve norms and inner products exactly. You lose nothing from the rotation itself. All the distortion comes from the scalar quantization step, which you've now made optimally efficient because you know the distribution analytically.

The Johnson-Lindenstrauss correction

There's a catch with MSE-optimal quantizers: they're biased for inner products. At 1 bit, the bias factor is $2/\pi$. Since attention is computed via inner products between queries and keys, bias is a problem — it systematically distorts which tokens the model attends to.

TurboQuant's second stage fixes this with the Quantized Johnson-Lindenstrauss transform (QJL). The idea: take the residual error from the first stage and encode it using a single sign bit via a random Gaussian projection. That's it. One bit per dimension, applied to the error, not the signal.

The JL lemma guarantees that random projections preserve inner products up to a controlled distortion. The quantized version preserves this guarantee even after reducing to sign bits. The combined estimator — MSE-optimal quantization plus 1-bit QJL correction — is provably unbiased:

$$\mathbb{E}[\langle y, \tilde{x} \rangle] = \langle y, x \rangle$$

with variance bounded by $\frac{\sqrt{3}\pi^2 \cdot \|y\|^2}{d} \cdot \frac{1}{4^b}$. At $b = 3$ bits total, that's vanishingly small.

This is what makes TurboQuant more than a quantization trick. It's a two-stage estimator with provable guarantees: the first stage minimizes reconstruction error, the second stage eliminates bias. The theoretical distortion is within $\frac{3\pi}{2} \approx 2.7\times$ of the Shannon lower bound across all bit-widths, narrowing to $1.45\times$ at 1 bit.

Why KV cache is where this matters most

The reason this is important right now is that KV cache is the binding constraint for long-context inference. A 70B parameter model serving 128K context windows can easily need 40+ GB just for the cache. That's often more than the weights themselves. Every technique that extends context length — and there's enormous commercial pressure to do so — makes this worse.

Weight quantization (GPTQ, AWQ, GGUF) has been well-explored and works well enough. But KV cache quantization has lagged because the cache is dynamic. You can't offline-optimize something that doesn't exist yet. TurboQuant's data-oblivious design sidesteps this entirely. The random rotation matrix and codebook are fixed at initialization. Each incoming vector quantizes independently via rotate $\to$ lookup $\to$ store. True streaming quantization.

The results bear this out. On needle-in-a-haystack retrieval, PolarQuant (the first stage alone, presented at AISTATS 2026) scores 0.991 vs. KIVI's 0.984 on Llama 3.1 8B, with $4.2\times$ compression. The full TurboQuant system pushes to $6\times$ compression at 3.5 bits per channel with what they call "absolute quality neutrality." On LongBench, PolarQuant at ~3.9 bits scores 48.37 vs. the exact 16-bit score of 48.63. KIVI manages 46.70.

What this means for small models on real devices

Here's where it connects to the work we've been doing with Qwen3 4B. Running a 4B model on-device is already tight. When you add long context, the KV cache becomes the limiting factor fast. A method that compresses cache $6\times$ with no accuracy loss and no training overhead isn't an incremental improvement — it's the difference between "fits in memory" and "doesn't."

TurboQuant is also fast to compute. Random rotation is a matrix multiply (and with structured random matrices like the randomized Hadamard transform, it's $O(d \log d)$ instead of $O(d^2)$). Codebook lookup is a table read. QJL is a sign operation. The whole pipeline is embarrassingly parallel and GPU-friendly. They report 4-bit TurboQuant running $8\times$ faster than unquantized 32-bit keys on H100. For edge devices, the gains would be different but the computational simplicity translates.

The data-oblivious property is arguably more important than the compression ratio for deployment. It means you don't need a calibration dataset per model. You don't need to re-quantize when you fine-tune. You don't even need to know what domain your inputs will come from. The quantizer is a mathematical object, not a learned one.

Honest assessment, revisited

I originally wrote five sharp critiques of this paper. Having dug into the KV-cache quantization literature more carefully — KIVI, GEAR, KVQuant, QServe, QuaRot, Atom, WKVQuant — I want to update them with evidence.

On the $8\times$ speedup. The headline number is 4-bit keys vs. 32-bit keys on H100. Every major serving framework (vLLM, TensorRT-LLM) defaults to fp16 or bf16 for KV cache. Against the production-relevant fp16 baseline, the speedup is $\sim 4\times$. KIVI, KVQuant, and Atom all benchmark against fp16 — that's the field's convention. But calling fp32 a "strawman" was too strong on my part. Google's internal JAX stack does use fp32 for some intermediate computations, and the paper reports results across multiple bit-widths (at 3 bits, you get $\sim 5.3\times$ even vs fp16). The honest framing: $4\times$ vs the baseline most people actually use, $8\times$ vs the one the paper chose. Still excellent either way.

On novelty as composition. I said TurboQuant is "just" composing PolarQuant (AISTATS 2026) and QJL (AAAI 2025), both of which existed separately, with overlapping authors. That's factually correct. But I understated the theoretical contribution. PolarQuant alone is biased for inner products. QJL alone is limited to 1 bit. The paper's real contribution is proving that the two-stage combination achieves near-optimal distortion rates — within $\frac{3\pi}{2} \approx 2.7\times$ of the Shannon lower bound at any bit-width — with a formal distortion-rate analysis. That's a stronger result than either component alone. QuaRot (NeurIPS 2024, open-source) had already used Hadamard rotations for KV cache quantization, so the rotation idea isn't new. But the distortion-optimality proof is.

On the $2.7\times$ Shannon gap. I framed this as "the cost of data-obliviousness," implying TurboQuant pays a price that smarter methods avoid. Technically true — KVQuant (NeurIPS 2024) uses calibration-based sensitivity-weighted non-uniform quantization and achieves $< 0.1$ perplexity degradation at 3 bits, while TurboQuant needs 3.5 bits for "absolute quality neutrality." But the framing was misleading. For KV cache, data-obliviousness isn't a cost — it's the requirement. You can't calibrate on tokens you haven't seen. KVQuant's calibration introduces its own fragility: if inference distribution diverges from calibration data, quality degrades. TurboQuant is robust by construction. And the gap narrows to $1.45\times$ at 1 bit, exactly where you need guarantees most. On actual LLM benchmarks (LongBench, needle-in-haystack), TurboQuant at 3.5 bits matches or exceeds KIVI at similar bit-widths. The distortion gap doesn't translate to a quality gap in practice.

On no open-source implementation. This remains a fair observation. As of March 2026, there's no official TurboQuant code on GitHub. Compare that to the competition: KIVI (ICML 2024), QuaRot (NeurIPS 2024), KVQuant (NeurIPS 2024), GEAR (NeurIPS 2024) — all open-source. Even QJL, by the same lead author, has code on GitHub. However, I was wrong to frame this as violating "Google's usual practice." Google is selective about what it open-sources, especially for systems that provide production serving advantages. TurboQuant likely powers Gemini/Gemma serving infrastructure. The paper was also just published — code may follow. But for now, every major competitor has open-source code and TurboQuant doesn't. That matters for adoption and reproducibility.

On cache-only quantization. TurboQuant handles KV cache but not model weights. I originally framed this as a limitation. Looking at the landscape, it's just how the field works. QServe (MLSys 2025) uses W4A8KV4 — different methods for weights (4-bit), activations (8-bit), and KV cache (4-bit). Atom (MLSys 2024) similarly combines separate quantization strategies for each component. WKVQuant explicitly shows that joint weight-KV quantization uses different methods for each. The bottleneck TurboQuant targets is the right one: for long-context inference, a 70B model at 128K context can have a 40+ GB KV cache, exceeding the $\sim$35 GB model weight footprint. And TurboQuant also applies to nearest-neighbor search — it outperforms product quantization (PQ, RabbiQ) on vector search benchmarks with zero indexing time — so "cache-only" actually understates its scope.

The connection I keep coming back to

There's something satisfying about the information-theoretic framing. The Shannon lower bound says: for $b$ bits per dimension, you cannot do better than $1/4^b$ distortion. TurboQuant gets within a constant factor of this without knowing anything about the data. That constant factor comes from the gap between the Beta distribution (what random rotation gives you) and the distribution that would be optimal for quantization (uniform, which gives exactly the Shannon bound).

For streaming KV cache, data-obliviousness isn't a tradeoff — it's the only viable approach. You can't run KVQuant-style calibration on tokens that haven't been generated yet. The $2.7\times$ gap is the price of operating in the real world, and in exchange you get streaming operation, zero calibration overhead, and theoretical guarantees that hold for any input. On LongBench, PolarQuant (the first stage alone) at $\sim$3.9 bits scores 48.37 vs KIVI's 46.70 and exact fp16's 48.63. The theory says you should lose something. The benchmarks say you don't.

There's a broader lesson here about when to use learned compression vs. geometric compression. Calibration-based approaches (KVQuant, KIVI) can be more bit-efficient when the inference distribution matches calibration data. Geometric approaches (TurboQuant, QuaRot) win when the data is streaming, non-stationary, or you need worst-case guarantees. KV cache is firmly in the second camp. The paper makes this distinction clearly and I wish more quantization work did.
