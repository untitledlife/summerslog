TL;DR

DiffuMamba replaces the Transformer backbone in masked diffusion language models with bidirectional Mamba-2 (a state space model with linear-time complexity). At 1.3B scale, it matches Transformer-based diffusion quality while being up to 8.2x faster on long sequences. The hybrid version (DiffuMamba-H, ~20% attention blocks interleaved with Mamba) is the sweet spot: better quality than both pure variants and 4.3x faster than pure Transformer diffusion.

Key insight: Mamba's linear scaling makes it a natural fit for diffusion LMs, which need to process the full sequence at every denoising step (no KV cache trick). Transformers pay quadratic cost per step × K steps. Mamba pays linear cost per step × K steps.

Connection to your work: They do NOT use block diffusion (BD3-LM) natively — they only test Fast-dLLM (training-free block decoding) post-hoc. They do NOT try RWKV. Your RWKV-BD3 hybrid is complementary and potentially stronger on the block diffusion front.

1. What's Mamba?

State Space Models (SSMs) — The Foundation

SSMs are sequence models derived from continuous-time dynamical systems. The core idea: maintain a hidden state h that evolves with each input token via learned transition matrices.

h_t = A · h_{t-1} + B · x_t
y_t = C · h_t + D · x_t

Key property: this recurrence can be computed as a convolution (parallelizable during training) or as a scan (sequential during inference), giving O(L) complexity vs Transformer's O(L²).

Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023)

Mamba's breakthrough: input-dependent (selective) state transitions. Unlike classic SSMs where A, B are fixed, Mamba makes them functions of the input. This lets the model selectively remember or forget information based on content, not just position.

  • Selective scan: A, B, C are computed per-token from a linear projection of the input
  • Hardware-aware algorithm: custom CUDA kernel that avoids materializing the full state, keeping memory O(L) instead of O(L·d_state)
  • Linear complexity: O(L) time and memory for length L sequences
  • No attention, no KV cache: fundamentally different compute pattern

Mamba-2 (Dao & Gu, 2024)

Mamba-2 connects SSMs to linear attention, showing they're equivalent under certain parameterizations. Uses structured state space duality (SSD) — can switch between recurrence and attention-like matrix multiplication depending on which is faster.

Why Mamba for Diffusion?

Autoregressive models process one token at a time — KV cache means each step is O(d), amortizing the quadratic cost. But diffusion models process the entire sequence at every denoising step (K steps total). No KV cache trick possible.

  • Transformer diffusion: O(K · L² · d) total compute
  • Mamba diffusion: O(K · L · d²) total compute

For long sequences (L > d), Mamba wins dramatically. This is why the speedup grows with sequence length: 8.2x at 65K+ tokens.

2. How Diffusion Works Here

Masked Diffusion with Absorbing States

DiffuMamba uses discrete masked diffusion (not continuous Gaussian diffusion). The forward process progressively replaces tokens with a special [MASK] token:

Forward: q(x_t | x_0) = ∏ Cat(x_t^i; (1-t)δ_{x_0^i} + t·δ_{MASK})

At t=0, you have the original text. At t=1, everything is [MASK]. The parameter t ∈ [0,1] controls the noise level — what fraction of tokens are masked.

The Denoising Task

The model learns to predict the original token at each masked position, given the partially-masked sequence:

p_θ(x_0 | x_t, t) = ∏ Cat(x_0^i; softmax(z_i))

This is essentially a cloze task (like BERT's MLM), but structured as a diffusion process with a proper probabilistic framework.

Reverse Process (Generation)

Start from fully masked sequence. At each step, unmask some tokens:

  1. Start with all [MASK] tokens (t=1)
  2. Model predicts what each masked token should be
  3. Unmask a fraction of tokens (move from t to s < t)
  4. Repeat for K denoising steps until t=0

The transition from noise level t to s is:

If x_t^i ≠ [MASK]: keep it (already unmasked)
If x_t^i = [MASK]: re-mask with probability s/t, unmask with probability (t-s)/t

How This Differs from Continuous Diffusion (Images)

Image diffusion adds Gaussian noise and predicts the noise (ε-prediction) or the clean image (x_0-prediction). Text diffusion can't do this because tokens are discrete — you can't add "a little noise" to the word "cat." Instead, absorbing state diffusion masks tokens entirely and the model predicts the original token. This is the MDLM / Sahoo et al. (2024) framework.

3. Bidirectionality

The Problem

Standard Mamba is causal (left-to-right only). This is perfect for autoregressive generation but terrible for diffusion: when denoising a masked sequence, the model needs to see context from both sides of each masked token.

The Solution: BiMamba

Two independent Mamba layers process the sequence in opposite directions:

Forward: h_i→ = A_f · h_{i-1}→ + B_f · x_i (left to right)
Backward: h_i← = A_b · h_{i+1}← + B_b · x_i (right to left)
Combined: h_i = h_i→ + h_i← (additive fusion)

Why Additive Fusion?

They tried additive (sum) fusion. This is the simplest approach that maintains stability. Each direction has its own learned kernels (A_f, B_f, A_b, B_b). The forward pass captures left context; the backward pass captures right context. Adding them gives each position symmetric bidirectional context.

Alternative approaches (concatenation, gating) would increase parameter count or add complexity. Additive fusion keeps the linear-time property intact.

Critical: This Doubles the Compute

BiMamba runs two full Mamba passes per layer. This is why DiffuMamba has slightly more parameters than DiffuTran at the same config (e.g., 0.29B vs 0.24B at the 240M scale). The throughput advantage comes from linear vs quadratic scaling, not from fewer operations per se.

4. Architecture Details

Block Structure

Each block follows a standard pre-norm residual pattern:

y_mixer = Mixer(AdaLN(x; τ_t)) + x
y_out = MLP(AdaLN(y_mixer; τ_t)) + y_mixer

Where Mixer is either BiMamba or MHA depending on the variant.

Noise Conditioning: Adaptive Layer Norm

The timestep t is embedded via a small MLP into τ_t, which modulates each layer through adaptive layer normalization:

AdaLN(x; τ_t) = γ_t · (x - μ) / σ + β_t
where (γ_t, β_t) = W_cond · τ_t

This is how the model knows what noise level it's denoising from.

5. Training: Loss & Objectives

The Loss Function

Masked diffusion loss (from Sahoo et al., 2024 — MDLM):

L_MDM = ∫_0^1 (1/t) · E_{q(x_t|x_0)} [∑_{i: x_t^i = MASK} -log p_θ(x_0^i | x_t)] dt

Breaking this down:

  • Outer integral: Average over all noise levels t ∈ [0,1]
  • 1/t weighting: Gives more weight to low-noise (easier) levels. This ensures the model learns to make fine-grained distinctions when most of the sequence is visible
  • Inner sum: Only compute loss at masked positions (like BERT MLM)
  • Cross-entropy: Standard categorical prediction at each masked position

Key Properties of This Objective

  • ELBO-based: This is a proper variational lower bound on log p(x_0), not just a heuristic training loss
  • Continuous-time: t is sampled uniformly from [0,1], not discrete steps
  • No noise schedule tuning: Uses fixed log-linear schedule, not cosine/linear that needs tuning
  • Same loss for all architectures: DiffuTran, DiffuMamba, DiffuMamba-H all use identical training objective — only the backbone changes

6. Block Diffusion (BD3-LM Connection)

They did NOT train with block diffusion. They only used Fast-dLLM (Wu et al., 2025) as a training-free post-hoc decoding strategy for block-autoregressive generation.

What They Actually Did

Fast-dLLM splits the sequence into blocks of size G=32 and denoises each block with k denoising steps, using KV-cache for inter-block conditioning:

  • Process blocks left-to-right (autoregressive at block level)
  • Within each block: parallel masked diffusion (k=32/p steps)
  • KV-cache stores the already-denoised blocks for conditioning
  • Recompute KV-cache after each block is finalized

How This Differs from BD3-LM

BD3-LM (Arriola et al., ICLR 2025 Oral): Trains with block structure from scratch. The model learns block-level autoregressive conditioning explicitly. Joint training of the block boundary transitions.

Fast-dLLM: Takes any full-sequence diffusion model and applies block decoding at inference time. No retraining needed. KV-cache handles inter-block dependencies post-hoc.

DiffuMamba only tested Fast-dLLM. They explicitly list "joint training with block cache reuse" as an unexplored limitation (Appendix B). This is a clear research gap.

Connection to your RWKV-BD3 work: You trained RWKV with BD3's block diffusion objective natively. DiffuMamba only tested the training-free version. A Mamba-BD3 or BiRWKV-BD3 with native block training could outperform both. Your RWKV-BD3 result (NLL 1.50 vs DIT-BD3 1.54 at 15M) already shows the backbone matters for BD3.

8. Inference: Steps, Tokens, Speed

How Many Denoising Steps?

Denoising steps K = L / p, where:

  • L = sequence length (tokens to generate)
  • p ∈ {8, 16} = step factor (higher = fewer steps = faster but lower quality)

Examples:

  • Generate 1024 tokens with p=16: K = 64 denoising steps
  • Generate 1024 tokens with p=8: K = 128 denoising steps
  • Generate 65536 tokens with p=16: K = 4096 denoising steps

Each step processes the full sequence (all L tokens). So total forward passes = K, each of size L.

Where the Speedup Comes From

They decompose latency as: t(L) = aL² + bL + c

  • a (quadratic term): DiffuTran has large a (attention). DiffuMamba has negligible a
  • b (linear term): Similar across models
  • c (constant): Minor

At short sequences (L < 2K), the quadratic term doesn't dominate yet, so DiffuTran is competitive. Beyond L > 2K, quadratic kicks in and Mamba pulls ahead monotonically.

Scaling Behavior

  • L ≤ 2K tokens: DiffuTran + Fast-dLLM has highest throughput (quadratic hasn't kicked in, attention is well-optimized by CUDA)
  • L > 2K tokens: DiffuMamba starts winning, gap grows with L
  • L > d (d=1920): Enters compute-saturated regime, Mamba's advantage maximized
  • L = 262K tokens: DiffuMamba 2.3x faster even with block caching

Hardware

Single NVIDIA H100, bf16, PyTorch + CUDA graphs. Batch size 1. Averaged over 5 runs (3 warm-up).

9. vs Other Diffusion Models & AR

vs AR Models

The paper does not provide direct head-to-head quality comparisons with AR models of the same size. They acknowledge diffusion models generally trail AR in raw perplexity. Their focus is on diffusion-vs-diffusion backbone comparison.

However, the throughput argument is clear: at batch size 1 and long sequences, diffusion + Mamba can exceed AR throughput because:

  • AR generates 1 token per step (memory-bandwidth bound)
  • Diffusion generates L/K tokens per step (compute-bound, better hardware utilization)

RWKV is NOT mentioned in DiffuMamba. They cite Mamba, S4, Hyena, and H3, but never discuss RWKV, xLSTM, RetNet, or other linear-complexity alternatives.

What They Say About Other Backbones

From their Limitations section (Appendix B): "Alternative linear-time token mixers [are] not explored." They explicitly acknowledge this gap.

Why This Matters for You

Your dfusion-lm experiments showed a 3-way backbone-objective crossover:

  • Transformer best at AR (NLL 1.25)
  • RWKV best at BD3 block diffusion (NLL 1.50 vs DIT 1.54)

DiffuMamba only tested Mamba for MDLM (where Mamba already has an advantage). They didn't test whether Mamba is also optimal for BD3 block diffusion. Your result suggests it might not be — RWKV could be better for BD3.
