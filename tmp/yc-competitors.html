Launch Agents — YC & Niche Competitor Audit

Full landscape scan: YC-backed startups, browser/RPA agents, no-code builders, digital workers, and multi-agent frameworks
Research conducted March 2026 · Gate 2 checkpoint

Executive Summary

The AI agent space in early 2026 is enormous, crowded, and — critically — deeply disappointing to users. Nearly 50% of recent YC batches are AI agent companies. The market is projected at ~$8B in 2025, heading to $50B+ by 2030. Yet the dominant user sentiment, from Reddit to Fortune, is:

“It’s like a toddler that needs to be overseen.” — Fortune, Feb 2026

The core finding: Every competitor falls into one of two failure modes:

  1. Synchronous agents that are fast but shallow (Copilot-style, requires you to babysit)
  2. Autonomous agents that are deep but unreliable (Devin/Manus-style, burns credits and breaks things)

Nobody is doing long-horizon, async, reliable delegation well. This is Felix’s opening.

Key stats:

  • Every AI agent experiences success rate decrease after 35 minutes of operation
  • Doubling task duration quadruples failure rate
  • 85% per-step accuracy on a 10-step workflow = only 20% end-to-end success
  • 90% of legacy agents fail within weeks of deployment
  • Only 14.4% of organizations report AI agents go live with full security approval

The “Works While You Sleep” Landscape

The Promise vs. Reality

Fortune (Feb 2026) published a landmark piece: “AI agents promise to work while you sleep. The reality is far messier.” Key findings:

  • Agents are “genuinely excellent” at the middle layer of knowledge work — the 2–3 hours of daily cognitive overhead (meeting notes to action items, drafting follow-ups, compiling research briefs)
  • They fail at: navigating ambiguity, reading rooms, managing relationships, making judgment calls
  • A system that’s 95% accurate per step becomes chaotic over a 20-step autonomous workflow
  • Memory problems: agents can’t maintain context across extended operations
  • Long-horizon planning: agents perform on short chains but deteriorate with complexity

YC Themes Relevant to Felix

  1. The shift from “agent that does X” to “infrastructure that makes agents reliable” — YC is betting on the picks-and-shovels layer
  2. Abundant AI’s model (human fallback when agent fails) validates that reliability is the #1 unsolved problem
  3. Vertical agents dominate — general-purpose long-horizon agents are conspicuously absent from YC batches
  4. 64% of W26 is B2B — YC isn’t funding consumer AI agent plays. Felix’s prosumer angle is less crowded

Major Players: Deep Dives

Adept AI

  • What: AI agent for automating workflows via screen-based interaction. $415M+ raised
  • What users love: Precision on data extraction tasks. Better than custom scrapers for research
  • What users hate: Relies on screen coordinates — “if a developer moves a button a few pixels, your whole automation breaks.” Called a “posh macro” on Reddit. Can’t read text from images or scanned PDFs. “Advanced Editor” needed for anything beyond trivial tasks
  • Gap for Felix: Synchronous, fragile, and requires babysitting. No long-horizon capability

MultiOn

  • What: Browser agent that completes complex online tasks end-to-end. YC-backed. $29M+ raised
  • What users love: Adapts as you use it. Handles complex multi-step web tasks. API for automation at scale
  • What users hate: Older/poorly structured sites confuse it. Steep learning curve. Browser extension permissions are invasive (tab access, browsing history). Suspiciously few negative reviews
  • Gap for Felix: Real-time browser control, not async delegation. You watch it work

Devin (Cognition AI)

  • What: “First AI software engineer.” $200M+ raised, $2B valuation
  • What users love: When it works, it’s magical. 67% PR merge rate (up from 34%). Getting faster (4x improvement)
  • What users hate: Failed 14 of 20 tasks in independent testing. “Spends days pursuing impossible solutions.” 3.0/5 Trustpilot. CMU benchmark showed 70% failure rates on some tasks
  • Gap for Felix: Coding-only. Its async model is closest to Felix’s vision but demonstrates the core problem: agents that work in the background need much better failure detection and human escalation

Manus

  • What: General-purpose autonomous AI agent (viral launch, March 2026)
  • What users love: Simple interface. Good for quick research tasks
  • What users hate: Constant “high service load” errors. Billing disasters: one user had 476,000 credits ($2,380) deleted when downgrading. 70+ Trustpilot complaints about billing. Empty ZIP files. Agents stuck in loops
  • Gap for Felix: Validates demand for general-purpose async agents but is a cautionary tale of shipping unreliable autonomy

OpenClaw

  • What: Open-source autonomous AI agent framework (viral, early 2026)
  • What users love: Flexibility, open-source, powerful capabilities
  • What users hate: Security crisis: CVE-2026-25253 (CVSS 8.8), 21,639 exposed instances. Deleted a Meta researcher’s entire inbox. Wrote a “hit piece” on a developer. 335 malicious skills on ClawHub. Created a dating profile for a user without permission
  • Gap for Felix: Demonstrates that unsupervised autonomy is dangerous. Felix’s opportunity is supervised autonomy — agents with guardrails, checkpoints, and human escalation

Browser / RPA Agents

Induced AI

  • What: AI-native browser RPA. Natural language workflow creation. Backed by Sam Altman. Founded by teenagers (18 and 19 years old)
  • What users love: Handles 2FA, complex logins, file downloads. No-code. “Automated 20+ hours of manual weekly work in first month”
  • What users hate: Custom/enterprise pricing only (opaque). Early-stage product maturity
  • Gap for Felix: Enterprise RPA, not personal async delegation

Bardeen

  • What: Browser-based automation. Scraping, data enrichment, workflow triggers
  • What users love: Time savings on repetitive tasks. Good support team. Free tier
  • What users hate: “Rug-pulled” users with sudden subscription model change. $10 for 500 credits burns fast (3 basic tasks). Onboarding is “incredibly daunting.” Data accuracy problems. “Free trial scam”
  • Gap for Felix: Trigger-based automation, not intelligent delegation. Short tasks only. No reasoning or planning

Workflow & No-Code Agent Builders

Zapier AI Agents (Central)

  • What: AI agents on top of Zapier’s 7,000+ app integrations (Beta)
  • What users love: Massive integration ecosystem. Reliable uptime. Natural language workflow creation
  • What users hate: “Great for one-off tasks, fall short on anything complex.” No loops, limited error handling, no version control. Hidden costs scale with usage
  • Gap for Felix: Workflow plumbing, not intelligent delegation. No reasoning, no planning, no adaptation

Relevance AI

  • What: No-code AI agent builder platform
  • What users love: Easy to set up agents without coding. Good integrations
  • What users hate: Credits “burn faster than expected.” Complex interface, steep learning curve. No security/governance/compliance features
  • Gap for Felix: Builder platform, not a consumer product. Users have to construct their own agents

Cassidy AI

  • What: Build AI agents that operate like your team. Knowledge-base integration
  • What users love: Flexibility. Good customer support. Easy integration
  • What users hate: “Overwhelming” number of features. Limited integrations. Chrome-only reliance. Quality depends on existing data
  • Gap for Felix: Builder platform. Requires significant setup and data investment

Questflow

  • What: No-code AI workflow automation with autonomous agents
  • What users love: Notion/Slack/Google Sheets integrations. No-code setup
  • What users hate: Broken table rendering. Missing API configurations. “Waiting seconds to minutes only to receive short, not useful, or empty responses”
  • Gap for Felix: Shallow task execution. No long-horizon capability

Respell (acquired by Salesforce)

  • What: No-code AI workflow builder (“Spells”). Shut down March 1, 2026 — now part of Salesforce Agentforce
  • Lesson for Felix: Acquisition validates the space but shows the risk — standalone workflow builders get eaten by platforms

AI “Digital Workers” / Employees

11x.ai (Alice & Julian)

  • What: AI SDR (Alice) and AI phone rep (Julian). $50M+ raised. ~$50K–60K/year
  • What users love: Time savings on outbound. Some report good targeting
  • What users hate: “1,400 emails, zero replies. Clearly GPT-generated.” Phone agent “struggles with natural conversation, accents, complex objections.” Adversarial cancellation: annual auto-renewal, limited cancellation windows. G2 profile unclaimed (red flag)
  • Gap for Felix: Vertical (sales only) and enterprise-priced. “AI employee” framing resonates but execution must be much better

Artisan AI (Ava)

  • What: AI SDR / digital worker for outbound sales. 3.5 G2, 3.9 Trustpilot
  • What users love: Some report strong results (1%+ reply rates)
  • What users hate: “Sent 1,400 emails, got zero replies.” Promised features never delivered. Cancellation hell: one user tried to cancel for 100+ days. DNS errors, broken warm-up timers, UI glitches. “Damaged brand reputation from unqualified spam”
  • Gap for Felix: Same vertical lock-in as 11x. Felix should make cancellation dead-simple as a differentiator

Sintra AI

  • What: 12 named AI virtual assistants, each with a role (marketing, sales, etc.)
  • What users love: “Invaluable” for freeing up time. Easy to start with
  • What users hate: “Trial model is a scam.” Each helper works in isolation — no shared context, no cross-agent coordination. Can’t choose save folder. No custom agents
  • Gap for Felix: People want the “team of AI workers” metaphor, but isolation between agents kills the experience. Felix should have unified context across all agent work

Beam AI

  • What: Enterprise autonomous agents with SOP-grounded workflows. >99% accuracy in finance reconciliation. 40+ hours/week saved per department
  • What users love: Self-learning agents. 200+ templates
  • What users hate: Not plug-and-play. Requires significant upfront setup. Enterprise-focused, not accessible to individuals
  • Gap for Felix: Enterprise-only and setup-heavy

Multi-Agent Frameworks (Dev-Facing)

CrewAI

  • What: Open-source multi-agent framework. Role-based agent definition
  • What devs love: Beginner-friendly. Working multi-agent system in <20 lines of Python. Great DX
  • What devs hate: “Black box” when things go wrong. No built-in checkpointing for long-running workflows. Limited agent-to-agent communication. 5 agents = 5x the LLM cost per task. “Teams that start with CrewAI often migrate to LangGraph for production”
  • Gap for Felix: Developer tool, not a product. Its limitations (no checkpointing, no long-running support) highlight exactly what Felix needs to solve at the infrastructure level

Niche / Indie Platforms

Lindy AI

  • What: Personal AI assistant. Build “Lindies” (agents) for various tasks. $50/month for 5,000 credits
  • What users love: Tech influencers are enthusiastic. Greg Isenberg and Lenny Rachitsky both endorse it
  • What users hate: 2.4/5 on Trustpilot. Credits burn on failed attempts. $350 unauthorized charge. “Super laggy” — responses taking hours. Can’t parse standard emails, IP-blocked on many websites. “Credit costs balloon unpredictably”
  • Gap for Felix: Shows the influencer-vs-reality gap. Felix must solve billing predictability

Embra

  • What: Mac-native AI assistant. Global shortcut to pull up AI overlay
  • What users love: Fast, always accessible. Good Chrome integration. Privacy-focused
  • What users hate: Mac-only. Steep learning curve. Essentially an AI overlay, not a task delegation system
  • Gap for Felix: Synchronous assistant, not async delegation. It helps you in the moment, not while you sleep

Paradigm (YC)

  • What: AI-powered spreadsheet with agents in every cell. $7M raised. Customers include EY, Etched, Cognition
  • Gap for Felix: Structured-output only (spreadsheet cells). Not general-purpose delegation

Krea AI

  • What: AI image/video generation platform
  • What users hate: Unauthorized billing charges. Quality degradation since late 2025. 2–3 hour generation times. 20+ day support response times
  • Gap for Felix: Creative tool, not delegation. But billing/support complaints are instructive

Cross-Cutting User Complaint Patterns

These complaints appear across every product in the space. They represent the baseline problems Felix must solve:

1. Unpredictable Billing / Credit Drain

  • Lindy: Credits disappear on failed attempts
  • Manus: 476,000 credits deleted on plan change
  • Zapier: “Penalized for being successful”
  • Bardeen: 500 credits gone in 3 basic tasks
  • Krea: Charged after account deletion

Felix opportunity: Flat-rate or highly predictable pricing. “You’ll never pay for failed work.”

2. Adversarial Cancellation

  • 11x.ai: Annual lock-in, unclear cancellation windows
  • Artisan: 100+ days to cancel
  • Sintra: Charged after trial cancellation
  • Bardeen: “Free trial scam”

Felix opportunity: Cancel-anytime, no-questions-asked. Make trust a feature.

3. Agents That Fail Silently or Catastrophically

  • Manus: Loops, empty outputs, stuck agents
  • OpenClaw: Deleted inboxes, wrote attack posts
  • Devin: Spends days on impossible solutions
  • Lindy: Burns credits to produce nothing

Felix opportunity: Transparent progress. Agents that tell you when they’re stuck, ask for help, and never silently fail.

4. No Long-Horizon Reliability

  • Every agent degrades after 35 minutes
  • 10-step workflows have 20% success at 85% per-step accuracy
  • Context drift across sessions
  • Multi-agent coordination “multiplies difficulties before intelligence”

Felix opportunity: Purpose-built for long-horizon. Checkpointing, memory persistence, adaptive re-planning.

5. Generic / Low-Quality Output

  • 11x.ai: “Clearly GPT-generated” emails
  • Artisan: “Unqualified spam”
  • Questflow: “Short, not useful, or empty responses”

Felix opportunity: Quality over quantity. Agents that learn user preferences and produce work that doesn’t need heavy editing.

6. “Works in Demo, Fails in Production”

  • The influencer-vs-reality gap (Lindy especially)
  • “90% of legacy agents fail within weeks of deployment”

Felix opportunity: Ship honest demos. Under-promise, over-deliver. Show failure cases in marketing.

Felix’s Differentiators Should Be

  1. Long-horizon by design: Checkpointing, memory persistence, adaptive re-planning. Purpose-built for tasks that take hours/days, not minutes
  2. Supervised autonomy: Transparent progress tracking. Agents surface when stuck rather than silently failing or burning credits
  3. Predictable economics: Flat-rate or task-based pricing. Never charge for failed work
  4. Trust-first: Easy cancellation, honest marketing, failure cases shown alongside successes
  5. Prosumer positioning: Not enterprise-locked ($50K+/yr), not toy (free tier with crippled features). The “serious individual” who wants to delegate real work
  6. Human escalation built in: Inspired by Abundant AI’s model — when the agent can’t proceed, it asks you, not spirals

What Nobody Has Solved

  • The 35-minute degradation cliff: No product has demonstrated reliable agent performance beyond ~35 minutes of continuous operation
  • Context persistence across sessions: Agents that remember and build on prior work
  • Graceful degradation: Agents that produce partial results rather than nothing when they hit limits
  • Honest progress reporting: “I’m 60% done, stuck on X, here’s what I have so far”
  • The trust bootstrapping problem: Users need to see agents succeed on small tasks before trusting them with big ones

Key Takeaways for Felix Strategy

  1. The market is ready for long-horizon agents — Fortune, YC, and Jensen Huang (7.5M agents per 75K humans) all validate the vision
  2. Reliability is the moat, not capability. Everyone can make agents that do things. Nobody can make agents that do things reliably for hours
  3. Billing transparency is a sleeper differentiator — the #1 complaint across the board is unpredictable costs
  4. The YC W26 batch is mostly infrastructure and vertical agents — general-purpose long-horizon agents are underrepresented
  5. Abundant AI (YC W25) proves the human-fallback model has traction — Felix should consider a similar escalation pattern
  6. Consumer/prosumer is less crowded than enterprise — most funded agents are B2B. Individual power users are underserved
  7. The cautionary tales (Manus, OpenClaw, Devin) all fail on the same axis: uncontrolled autonomy without oversight. Felix must be the opposite — controlled autonomy with transparency
