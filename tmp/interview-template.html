User Interview Template

Launch Agents — Track 5 — Structured format for consistent, comparable interviews

Before you start: This interview should take 15–20 minutes. You're here to listen, not pitch. No solutions, no features, no "what if we built X." Pure problem discovery. Record if they're comfortable with it.

The goal: understand their pain well enough that you could write their daily frustration in their own words.

Phase 1 — Context (2 min)

Set the scene

Q0: Opening

"I'm trying to understand how people like you get work done day-to-day, especially the stuff that's annoying or falls through the cracks. There are no right answers. I just want to hear how things actually work, not how they're supposed to work."

Why this matters: Frame it as observation, not evaluation. People describe ideal workflows unless you explicitly ask for messy reality.
Phase 2 — Current Reality (5-7 min)

How they work today

Q1: Walk me through yesterday.

"Think about yesterday specifically. From when you started working to when you stopped. What did you actually spend your time on?"

Follow-ups: "What took longer than it should have?" / "Was there anything you put off?" / "What interrupted you?"
Why: Yesterday is concrete. "Usually" gives you aspirational answers. You want the real schedule, not the idealized one.

Q2: What's the thing that keeps piling up?

"Is there a type of task that you keep pushing to tomorrow? Something that's always on the list but never quite gets done?"

Follow-ups: "How long has it been piling up?" / "What happens when it finally gets too big to ignore?" / "Who notices when it doesn't get done?"
Why: The tasks people avoid are often the exact shape of the problem. Piling up = real pain, not hypothetical.

Q3: Tools and workarounds.

"What tools do you actually use to get through your day? And I mean actually use, not 'we have a license for.'"

Follow-ups: "Which one do you open first in the morning?" / "Do you use any AI tools? How?" / "What's your most janky workaround right now?"
Why: Current tools reveal the shape of the problem. AI usage level tells you their baseline comfort with automation.
Phase 3 — Delegation (5-7 min)

How they hand off work

Q4: Last time you delegated something, how did it go?

"Think of the last time you handed something off to someone, whether a person, a tool, whatever. What was it, and how did it go?"

Follow-ups: "Did you have to check on it? How many times?" / "What would have happened if they got it wrong?" / "Would you delegate it the same way again?"
Why: Concrete delegation stories reveal trust thresholds, failure tolerance, and how much oversight they need. This directly maps to the Level 1-5 delegation spectrum.

Q5: What would you never hand off?

"Is there anything you'd never let someone else do, even if they were really good? Why?"

Follow-ups: "Is that because they'd get it wrong, or because doing it is valuable to you?" / "Has that changed over time?"
Why: Distinguishes "I don't trust anyone to do this" from "doing this is part of my identity/learning." Both are non-ICP signals, but for different reasons. (Deepanshi won't delegate research because it makes her smarter. That's not a trust problem.)

Q6: The overnight test.

"Imagine you could tell someone exactly what you need done tonight, go to sleep, and wake up to it finished. What would you hand off?"

Follow-ups: "How would you know if they did it right?" / "What's the first thing you'd check in the morning?" / "What would make you nervous about that?"
Why: This is the core Launch Agents use case. Their answer here is the product. Pay attention to: what they pick (scope), how they'd verify (trust), and what makes them nervous (failure mode).
Phase 4 — Cost & Stakes (3-4 min)

How much it actually hurts

Q7: What's the cost of the status quo?

"If nothing changes about how you work today, what does that cost you? I mean in real terms: time, money, energy, whatever."

Follow-ups: "How many hours a week?" / "Does it affect your evenings/weekends?" / "Have you ever dropped something important because you were juggling too much?"
Why: Quantified pain is what makes Gate 1 compelling. "It's annoying" is not enough. "I lose 8 hours a week to coordination" is.
Phase 5 — Close (1 min)

Wrap up

Q8: Anything I should have asked?

"Is there anything about how you work, or what frustrates you, that I didn't ask about but should have?"

Why: The best insights often come here. People have been thinking about their pain for 15 minutes and usually have something they've been waiting to say.

Post-Interview: What to Capture

Within 5 minutes of ending, write down:

  1. One sentence: their core pain in their own words
  2. Delegation level: where do they operate on the 1-5 spectrum?
  3. Overnight test answer: what did they pick?
  4. Trust threshold: low (try anything), medium (need proof first), high (need to see it work 10 times)
  5. Current AI usage: none / basic chat / daily tool / power user
  6. Quantified cost: hours/week, money, or specific dropped balls
  7. ICP fit: strong / maybe / not a fit — and why
  8. Best quote: the one line that captures their experience
