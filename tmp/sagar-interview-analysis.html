1. The Product Thesis

A real-time AI copilot for people who extract information from other people in structured conversations. Not a meeting note-taker. Not a transcript summarizer. A tool that sits beside the interviewer, tracks the agenda, suggests follow-up questions, flags gaps, and generates structured output afterward.

The key reframe: this is not a general "meeting AI" product. It's a tool for a specific act — the interview — where one person has an agenda and needs to systematically extract information from another person.

Target personas: user researchers, UX researchers, product managers doing customer discovery, focus group moderators, journalists, recruiters, investigators, consultants running discovery sessions.

2. What Ritam's Interview Actually Revealed

Sagar interviewed Ritam about his meeting habits. Ritam is not the ICP for this product — he has 3-4 meetings/week, zero IRL, mostly unstructured brainstorms. But the interview surfaced important signals anyway.

Key signals

Signal 1 — Underprepared and Fine With It

Said: "I am not prepared but I don't feel under prepared."

Means: Ritam is not the target user. The ICP is someone who does feel underprepared — someone running back-to-back user interviews who doesn't have time to review the discussion guide, check what was learned in previous sessions, or prepare follow-up questions from last time.

Signal 2 — Felt Lost Without an Agenda

Said: "That is exactly what happened yesterday. I asked like, what is your day look like? I had no plan about what question to ask."

Means: Even Ritam, who doesn't care about meeting prep, felt lost in a structured interview without a script. This validates the core premise: interviews are fundamentally different from meetings. In a meeting, you can free-flow. In an interview, you need structure or you waste everyone's time.

Signal 3 — "In Theory" = Will Not Use

Said: "In theory, it seems like very useful" (said twice about real-time suggestions).

Means: Ritam is telling you he won't use this. But that's fine — he's not the ICP. A UX researcher doing their 5th interview of the day, trying to remember what they already asked in interview 3, would have a very different reaction. The "in theory" response is specific to Ritam's low meeting volume and high context-switching tolerance.

Signal 4 — The "Done State" Problem

Said: "If I have an agenda of things and then if I see that these things across out during the meeting" — he wants visual confirmation that agenda items were covered.

Means: The interviewer wants to know: did I cover everything? This is the core UX. A real-time checklist that tracks which agenda points were addressed in conversation — not by the interviewer manually checking them off, but by the AI detecting coverage from the transcript. This is technically hard but extremely valuable.

3. Who Actually Needs This

Primary ICPs (high pain, high frequency)

PersonaInterview VolumePain PointCurrent Solution
UX Researcher 5–8/day during discovery sprints Repeating questions already answered, missing follow-ups, 2+ hours of synthesis per session Dovetail ($300+/mo), manual notes, Miro boards
Product Manager (discovery) 2–5/week during problem-validation Running interviews without a research background, forgetting to probe deeper, inconsistent interview quality Google Docs templates, maybe Grain
Recruiter / Hiring Manager 5–15/week Evaluating candidates consistently, remembering to cover all competencies, writing structured scorecards Greenhouse/Lever scorecards, manual notes
Focus Group Moderator 1–3/week (but high stakes) Managing multiple participants, tracking who said what, ensuring all topics covered Discussion guides + assistant moderator (human)
Journalist Variable (1–5/week) Following up on unexpected threads, fact-checking claims in real-time, not missing the story Notebook + experience + Otter for transcription

Why UX researchers are the best launch ICP

  • High volume: 5–8 interviews per day during discovery means the tool gets used constantly, not occasionally.
  • Structured process: They already use discussion guides, which means there's a clear agenda to track against.
  • Painful synthesis: Post-interview synthesis is the #1 time sink. They spend 2x the interview duration on notes, tagging, and coding. Automation here is worth paying for.
  • Existing budget: UX research teams already spend $300–500+/mo on Dovetail, Maze, or UserTesting. There's willingness to pay.
  • Clear done-state: "Did I cover all my discussion guide questions?" is a yes/no that the AI can track.

5. The Gap Nobody Is Filling

The entire interview copilot space is built for the interviewee. Final Round AI has 10M+ users getting real-time help answering questions. Nobody is building the equivalent for the person asking the questions.

Meanwhile, the research tools space (Dovetail, Marvin, Looppanel) is entirely post-hoc. They help you analyze after the interview. Nobody helps you during the interview.

The whitespace: a real-time copilot for the interviewer. Not the interviewee. Not post-hoc. During the conversation, helping the person whose job is to extract information.

What this tool would do:

  1. Before: Ingest the discussion guide or agenda. Surface context from previous interviews with this person or on this topic. Generate suggested probing questions based on research goals.
  2. During: Track which agenda items have been covered (detected from transcript, not manual checkboxes). Suggest follow-up questions when the interviewee says something interesting but the interviewer doesn't probe. Flag contradictions with previous interviews. Stay silent unless genuinely useful.
  3. After: Generate structured output — not a transcript summary, but a filled-in version of the discussion guide with quotes mapped to each question. Theme extraction across multiple interviews. Exportable to Dovetail/Notion/Google Docs.

6. Market Evidence

The numbers

  • UX Research Software market: $2.4B in 2026, growing at 15.4% CAGR. Driven by AI integration and remote research adoption.
  • AI meeting assistant market: Crowded (50+ tools) but commoditizing fast. Transcription is table stakes. Differentiation is moving upstream (pre-meeting) and downstream (integration with workflows).
  • Interview copilot demand signal: Final Round AI alone has 10M+ users. Even if 90% are job seekers cheating, the remaining 10% signal genuine demand for real-time conversational assistance.
  • Video-based interview tools: 46% of all research activity in 2026. The medium is video calls. The tooling should live there.
  • Researcher pain: 48% of platforms now offer automated sentiment classification, but only 46% of orgs actually use AI-assisted qualitative analysis. Adoption is lagging capability — meaning the tools aren't solving the real problem yet.

Willingness to pay

  • Dovetail charges $300–500+/mo for enterprise plans. UX research teams have budget.
  • Granola charges $18/mo for unlimited meetings. Individual researchers will pay for convenience.
  • Final Round AI charges $99–149/mo for interview copilot features. Users pay because the stakes are high (job offers).
  • For an interviewer-side copilot: $20–50/mo for individuals, $100–200/seat/mo for teams is plausible if the real-time features work.

7. Opinionated Takes & Counter-arguments

Take 1: This is a real gap, not an imagined one

Verdict: Yes, build this

The interviewer-side copilot space is genuinely empty. The interviewee-side is booming (Final Round AI proves demand exists for real-time conversational AI). The post-hoc research tools are well-funded but stuck in the "analyze after" paradigm. Nobody is sitting beside the interviewer during the conversation. This is a real product gap with evidence of demand on the other side of the table.

Take 2: "Silent by default" is the make-or-break feature

Verdict: This is the hardest engineering problem

Ritam's most important reaction: he engaged most when Sagar described the tool as "mostly silent, only speaks when you genuinely need something." This is right. But building a system with high precision (only surfaces when genuinely useful) and high recall (doesn't miss the moments that matter) is extremely hard. Every competitor that's tried real-time suggestions has either set the bar too low (noisy, annoying, users disable it) or too high (never fires, users forget it exists).

The solution for v1: Don't try to be smart about when to suggest. Instead, use a simple heuristic: show the agenda checklist at all times, highlight uncovered items, and only surface follow-up suggestions when the user explicitly glances at the panel (gaze detection) or clicks "suggest." Make it pull-based, not push-based. Push can come in v2 when you have enough data to know when to speak.

Take 3: The post-interview output is where you win, not the during-interview features

Verdict: Counterintuitive but probably true

The sexy pitch is "real-time AI copilot during your interview." But the actual pain that researchers will pay to eliminate is the 2 hours of synthesis after each interview. If your tool produces a structured, filled-in discussion guide with mapped quotes, theme tags, and cross-interview patterns — that's worth $50/mo to every UX researcher on the planet. The during-interview features are the hook. The post-interview output is the retention driver.

Counter-argument: Dovetail and Marvin already do post-interview analysis. If you only compete on post-hoc features, you're entering a crowded space with well-funded incumbents. The during-interview copilot is the differentiator that justifies switching costs. You need both, but the during-interview part is what makes you different, even if the post-interview part is what makes you useful.

Take 4: Standalone beats Felix integration for this use case

Verdict: Ritam is right

Ritam's instinct to decouple this from Felix is correct. The ICP (UX researchers, PMs, journalists) doesn't use Felix. They use Zoom, Google Meet, and Dovetail. A standalone tool that integrates with those platforms has a much larger addressable market than a Felix module. Felix integration can come later as a distribution channel.

Counter-argument: Building standalone means building auth, billing, a landing page, onboarding, and support — all things that Felix already has. The sprint is 5 days. A Felix module ships faster. But for the product vision, standalone is the right call. For the sprint demo, a Felix-integrated version is more practical.

Take 5: The Suits analogy is the right mental model

Verdict: This should be the product narrative

Sagar referenced Suits — Harvey Specter and Mike Ross. The interviewer is Harvey (leading the conversation, making the calls). The AI is Mike (has perfect recall, surfaces the right document at the right time, never interrupts unless it matters). This is a compelling narrative for marketing: "Your Mike Ross for every interview." It's specific, memorable, and communicates the value proposition instantly.

More importantly, it sets the right expectation: Mike doesn't talk during Harvey's client meetings. He prepares the brief, slides a note when needed, and writes up the summary afterward. That's exactly the behavior pattern this tool should have.

8. What to Build, What to Skip

For the sprint demo (3 days of coding)

  1. Agenda tracker with coverage detection. User pastes a discussion guide. The tool listens to the conversation (via transcript stream) and highlights which questions have been covered. This is the simplest, most demonstrable feature. Shows the core value in 30 seconds.
  2. Follow-up question suggestions. When the interviewee gives a short or surface-level answer, suggest a probing question. Use a simple heuristic: if the answer is under 20 words and the topic is flagged as important, suggest "Can you tell me more about that?" or a more specific probe. Don't try to be clever — be useful.
  3. Post-interview structured output. After the call, generate a filled-in discussion guide with the interviewee's answers mapped to each question, including direct quotes. This is the "aha" moment for UX researchers.

For the demo narrative

Use Sagar's own interview with Ritam as the demo case. Show the tool tracking the agenda in real-time, highlighting that 3 of 8 questions were covered organically, suggesting 2 follow-ups that Sagar missed, and producing the structured output at the end. Meta and compelling.

What to skip

  • Real-time link/document surfacing. Too hard to get right in 3 days. The precision bar is too high. Save for v2.
  • Cross-interview pattern detection. Requires multiple interviews in the system. Can't demo with one interview. Save for v2.
  • Integration with Dovetail/Linear/Notion. Export to JSON/CSV is enough for the demo. Real integrations take weeks.
  • IRL meeting support. Different engineering problem (ambient mic, no screen to show the copilot on). Virtual-first.

Validation after the sprint

  • Find 3 UX researchers and show them the demo. Their reaction will tell you everything.
  • Ask: "Would you pay $30/mo for this?" Not "do you like it?" but "would you pay?"
  • Test the silence problem: in a real interview, how often does the tool suggest something useful vs something distracting? Track the ratio. Below 30% useful = the feature is net-negative.
