Synthesis Interviews & ICP All Insights Cyril Deepanshi Kautuk Ritam Sagar Sahil

Interview Insights: Sahil

iOS Developer, Conscious Engines — Single round

1. Profile

Role: iOS developer at Conscious Engines. Hands-on coder who uses Claude Code daily. Has a side product he tracks analytics for. Works with Linear for task management.

Context: Very low delegation profile — does almost everything himself. Ritam identified him during the interview as likely not the ICP: "I think you are almost not ICP for us."

Interview date: March 25, 2026 (recorded 15:06, single round).

2. Delegation Philosophy

Delegation ratio: 99/1. Less than 5% of tasks are delegatable, maybe as low as 1%. He does nearly everything himself.

"Those things will be like less than 5%, maybe like 1 percent."

Delegation style is informal pseudo-delegation. He writes other people's names in his to-do list as reminders, but doesn't actually assign or notify them. It's mental bookkeeping, not real delegation.

"It doesn't tag them. It's just regular text. But I just write it like that."

Correction loop mirrors prompting. When delegation does happen and comes back wrong, he compares the correction process to prompt engineering: "You would ask them to change... just like prompt them to nudge them in the correct direction."

Already using AI for ~80% of coding. The coding delegation angle is mostly solved for him.

3. Key Insights

  1. Explaining tasks is the bottleneck, not identifying them. Without hesitation: "Explaining is 100%."

    "Explaining is 100%."

  2. Claude Code's silent failure is his worst experience. Lost 20+ minute blocks multiple times because Claude stalled on a confirmation prompt he didn't see.

    "I thought it was coding... I thought it will do it, but for some reason it failed... I thought it will at least alert me."

  3. Wants proactive nudge notifications. Claude sits idle for 1–2 hours waiting for input. It should detect non-response and re-ping.

    "[Claude] is so intelligent that it can do all this coding, but it does not know that I didn't see it. That's why I'm not replying. So it just stays there for like 1 or 2 hours."

  4. Trust threshold is high: ~1 week / ~100 tasks. 5 successes is not enough. He needs sustained reliability over roughly a week before he stops checking.

    "5 times is not enough, but I think eventually I will become less and less... Like a 100 tasks... over like a week or something."

  5. Research delegation makes him feel dumb. He values the serendipity of research — adjacent discoveries you stumble on while searching. AI summaries strip that away.

    "When you're searching for things, adjacent things... you don't put those things in the research, but you still know that this exists."

  6. Proactive Linear status updates. His concrete pain: manually marking Linear tickets done after opening PRs. Wants the system to detect the PR and update automatically.

    "If I open PR for that thing... I manually go and mark it done."

  7. WhatsApp is where real context lives. Tasks and context arrive via WhatsApp but can't flow into his task management system. He wants to forward WhatsApp messages to his to-do list.

    "A lot of stuff happens on WhatsApp... if I can just forward it to my to-do list."

  8. Morning debrief over real-time monitoring. Does not want to watch the agent work in real-time. Wants async summaries.

    "Morning debrief. I don't want to look at [it working in real-time]."

  9. Would never let AI send money autonomously. Hard boundary. The agent can raise payment requests, but he must approve.

    "I would never feel comfortable asking the AI to send money to somebody... It can raise the payment, but I have to approve it."

  10. 2 hours reclaimable per day. Works 7–8 hour days; estimates 2 hours could have been done overnight. When framed as "you lost 2 hours this morning doing work that could have been done while you sleep," it stings.

4. Pain Points

  • Silent failures in Claude Code. Lost 20+ minutes multiple times to Claude stalling on a prompt without notification. The single biggest frustration.
  • No proactive re-engagement. Claude waits 1–2 hours without checking if the user saw its message. Should re-notify.
  • Manual Linear status updates. Manually marking tickets done after PRs is tedious busywork.
  • Context fragmentation across WhatsApp. Tasks arrive via WhatsApp but can't flow into task management.
  • Research busywork in early phases. The initial Google/feasibility research phase before coding is tedious.
  • Explaining tasks is harder than deciding. The articulation bottleneck prevents delegation.

5. Trust Model

Dimension How Sahil Builds/Loses Trust
Building trust ~100 tasks over ~1 week. Gradually stops checking. Much higher bar than others (who said ~5 successes).
Failure response When delegation fails (to brother, to team), sometimes gives up and does it himself.
Trust death Destructive actions: sending weird messages to important people, deleting important files permanently.
Financial boundary Hard no on autonomous money transfers. Approval gate required. "That is a maximum."
Monitoring preference Morning debrief, not real-time. Does not want to watch the agent work.
Proactive expectations Wants the agent to re-notify when stuck waiting for input, not sit idle.

6. What He'd Delegate Tonight

  • Overnight research + POC implementation — research if a feature is feasible, figure out APIs, scaffold a proof of concept. Ready for morning review.
  • Linear ticket status updates — automatic status changes when PRs are opened.
  • WhatsApp-to-task-list forwarding — capture context from WhatsApp into task management.

7. Dealbreakers

  • Destructive actions — sending wrong messages to important people, deleting important files permanently
  • Silent failures (the Claude Code stalling story)
  • Autonomous financial actions without approval gate
  • Agent sitting idle without re-notifying when stuck

8. ICP Classification

Anti-ICP (for core product) / Moderate (for developer-specific features)

Reasoning: Sahil delegates almost nothing (1–5% of tasks). His work is deeply hands-on coding where he's already using AI for 80%. There is very little delegation surface area for a launch agent. Ritam explicitly identified him as "almost not ICP for us" during the interview.

However: Sahil reveals critical design constraints that apply to all users. His silent failure pain point is the most vivid of all interviewees. His proactive nudging request is a unique and valuable feature insight. His overnight research + POC use case shows that even low-delegation developers have a narrow but real delegation wedge. His ~100-task trust threshold is the highest measured, setting a useful upper bound for product design.

Why anti-ICP for core: The product needs users who have a large surface area of delegatable tasks. Sahil's is tiny. Building for him would mean over-optimizing for developer workflow automation (Linear integration, PR-to-ticket mapping) rather than the core launch agent value proposition of long-horizon autonomous work.
