Interview Insights: Cyril

Ops Lead, Conscious Engines — Rounds 1 & 2 combined

1. Profile

Role: Ops Lead — handles HR, engineering setup (CMS), compliance, partnerships with AI labs, procurement, payroll, hiring, leave policies, and "everything in between."

Context: His pain is breadth, not depth. He IS the coordination layer — the person who holds context across every domain at the company.

Interview dates: Round 1 — March 24, 2026. Round 2 — March 25, 2026 (~44 min).

2. Delegation Philosophy

Cyril created the Level 1–5 delegation spectrum that became the team's shared framework:

  • Level 1: Outcome + exact process. Pure automation.
  • Level 2: Outcome + most of the process, person fills small gaps.
  • Level 3: Outcome + rough process, agent adds judgment. The sweet spot for launch agents.
  • Level 4: Outcome only, person designs the process.
  • Level 5: Outcome only, person figures out the problem itself.

Delegation ratio: 70/30. 70% of his work is delegatable (repeatable process tasks). 30% is personal judgment that must stay with him.

Cyril operates at Level 5 himself — he gets outcomes and figures everything out. He wants to delegate at Level 3 (outcome + rough process, agent adds judgment), but ideally at Level 5 too. Delegation is a 2D model for him: task complexity AND who he is handing it to (trust x experience).

3. Key Insights

  1. Memory failure = trust death. Cyril tried Claude for a content plan (tweets, personal branding). Next session: forgot everything, talked in proper English instead of lowercase, lost the plan. He gave up entirely.

    "If you don't have a mechanism to consume feedback and remember it, there's no point."

  2. Payroll as perfect delegation example. A 10-step process (contracts → leave tracking → DM/email reimbursements → INR tally → per-bank forex rates → Mercury transfer). Pure process, zero judgment. Currently all manual.
  3. 80% serial >> 80% random. Steps 1–8 of 10 done sequentially = great. Random 8 of 10 where you figure out which 2 are missing = disaster.

    "If I am just the lego piece that connects two arms, I'm fine. If I have to figure out where I fit, I'm not."

  4. Test mode vs production mode. Patient and iterative during testing, zero tolerance in production. Will teach and provide feedback during test mode. In production, errors = fired.
  5. Specificity > generality. Agents that declare specific capabilities beat those that present an empty canvas.

    "If an agent says 'I can do everything' — give me an empty field? Your job is to figure out what you can do best."

  6. Reversibility framework. Two-way doors: delegate freely (calendar events, research, drafts). One-way doors: must be in the loop (money transfers, contracts, communications).
  7. Silent failure is the cardinal sin. Hierarchy: does it well > fails but notifies > fails but surfaces for async review > fails silently.

    "People get fired over that."

  8. Trust is gradual + measurable. 5 correct in a row → stops checking. For research: must capture everything he already knows. Missing known items = trust broken. Extra noise = tolerable.
  9. Force multiplier, not replacement. Wants to stay in the value chain. AI surfaces info → he forms inferences → he communicates. Full replacement makes him feel dumber.

    "That's a me problem, not an AI problem."

  10. Level 3 is where every current solution fails. Automation can't judge. Humans lose context. Current AI agents hallucinate or burn credits. The person who occupies Level 3 today is the founder, at 2 AM.

4. Pain Points

  • Memory loss across sessions. He manually rebuilds context every time he uses an AI tool. Quit Claude over this.
  • Breadth overload. Too many domains (HR, ops, compliance, procurement, partnerships) with no one else to hold the full context.
  • Generic agents that don't declare capabilities. Blank canvases create friction, not freedom.
  • Sequential completeness gaps. Getting random pieces of a process done, then having to figure out what's missing, is worse than doing it himself.
  • Dumb time sinks. 20 minutes across 3 e-commerce apps looking for Diet Coke, then went to buy it in person.

8. ICP Classification

Strong ICP

Reasoning: Cyril is the ideal non-technical ICP. His work is high-breadth with many repeatable process tasks (70% delegatable). He already thinks in delegation frameworks (created the Level 1–5 spectrum). His pain is being the only person who holds cross-domain context. He wants Level 3–5 delegation from an agent with memory, reversibility awareness, and loud failure modes. His tasks (payroll, procurement, HR automation, competitor research) map directly to what a launch agent could do.

Why strong, not moderate: He has concrete, recurring tasks with clear processes. He's willing to invest in test mode. His trust model is demanding but well-defined. He already tried to delegate to AI and quit because of memory — which means the product just needs to solve that one problem to win him back.
