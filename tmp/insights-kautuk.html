Interview Insights: Kautuk

Founder / CEO, Conscious Engines — Rounds 1 & 2 combined

1. Profile

Role: Solo founder / CEO. Also handles backend. Ex-Ethereum Foundation R&D. Founded Stackr Labs with a16z crypto. Deeply technical.

Context: The bottleneck. Things wait on him because nobody else has enough context to move them forward. No co-founder to share the cognitive load. Simultaneously sets research direction, writes code, and runs the company.

Interview dates: Round 1 — March 24, 2026 (via Ritam summary). Round 2 — March 25, 2026 (~15 min).

2. Delegation Philosophy

Delegation ratio: 80/20 (inverted). 80% of his work must be done personally (with AI help). Only 20% is delegatable to other people (engineering team tasks).

Already delegating coding to AI at an 80/20 split — AI handles most coding, saving him 50–60% of his time. What remains for him: research ideation, product thinking, strategic judgment.

The 80% threshold:

"If all his tasks were done at least 80%, he'd be happy."

Speed beats polish. He will do the last 20% himself. Prefers 80% done by morning over 90% done by afternoon.

The solo founder trap: When delegation fails, he absorbs the work rather than iterating. Explaining "how" is harder than deciding "what." The cost of delegation feels higher than doing it himself because he is fast and context-rich.

3. Key Insights

  1. Already deep into AI delegation for coding. This is not a prospect — it's infrastructure he depends on. AI saves him 50–60% of his coding time.

    "For most of my coding tasks I delegate to AI. So I am already doing there."

  2. Overnight autonomous agents — enthusiastic yes. The solo founder's dream: a team that works while he sleeps.

    "Imagine your agent did 5 tasks overnight, and you woke up with a summary, what it did... how comfortable is that? I love it."

  3. Control anxiety he is actively working through. As a technical founder who understands every layer, letting go of visibility requires deliberate effort.

    "There's kind of uncomfortable if I don't know what is happening. But I'm getting used to that feeling."

  4. Trust builds through process visibility, not just outcome. Requires 5 successful runs with visible process. Verifiability matters — ex-Ethereum Foundation researcher thinks in verifiable process.

    "I need to know the steps that have gone through in the first 5 times... not just did it 5 times."

  5. Binary trust transition. High oversight for first N runs, then full autonomy once trust is established. Not gradual — binary.

    "If I'm confident enough that the AI agent can do the task I can just delegate that task and never think about it."

  6. Has been burned by silent failures. Claude agent waiting for input while he thought it was working. Wasted significant time.

    "I thought my agent is working on something. But it was actually waiting for my input. And I wasted a lot of my time there."

  7. The "stay sharp" concern. If AI does research and keeps him accountable to read it, he feels sharper. If he stops engaging, he stagnates. For a founder whose edge is deep technical insight, this is existential.

    "If AI do my research and keep me accountable to at least go through those research then I'll feel sharper."

  8. Builder's empathy creates tolerance. Blames himself for agent failures because he understands the system's limitations. But recognizes paying customers won't have that grace.

    "Mostly it is my fault because I understand why it didn't work. But for a product, if it fails and it's paying for it, then I might not tolerate it."

  9. Personal/creative tasks are a hard boundary. Will not delegate messages, drawings, or anything with heart in it.

    "Something that I don't want AI to do is... message my girlfriend on my behalf. Draw things for someone... mostly creative things."

  10. Launch agents as queue-drainers. His pain is being the bottleneck. Tasks pile up not because they're hard, but because they're waiting for his attention. Agents take tasks from 0% to 80%, he reviews and ships.

4. Pain Points

  • Being the bottleneck. Tasks wait on him because nobody else has full context. Queue problem, not complexity problem.
  • Silent stalls. Agent waiting for input with no notification. Lost 20+ minutes before discovering the stall.
  • Explaining "how" is tedious. He knows what needs doing but articulating steps for others is draining.
  • Meetings feel unproductive. His leverage is in building, not in synchronous communication.
  • Personal brand procrastination. Knows he should build his personal brand but the activation energy is too high.
  • Black box failures. If he can't see the workflow and the product fails, he will stop using it.

5. Trust Model

Dimension How Kautuk Builds/Loses Trust
Building trust 5 successful runs with visible process. Must see the "workshop" it follows, not just results.
Trust transition Binary, not gradual. High oversight → then full fire-and-forget. No middle ground.
Failure tolerance Higher when workflow is visible ("I can see where it is failing"). Near-zero when process is a black box.
Builder's empathy Blames himself for failures because he understands the system. But recognizes this won't scale to paying users.
Trust death Black box + any failure rate. Silent stalls/skips. Opaque failure modes.
Fail-loud preference "Obviously fails and telling you immediately is the way to go." Silent skips are worse than failures.

6. What He'd Delegate Tonight

  • Personal brand building — content strategy, drafts, posting cadence. He's procrastinating because activation energy is high. Perfect for AI bootstrapping.
  • Research surfacing and summarization — already happening with AI, could be more structured and autonomous.
  • Coding implementation — already delegated to AI but could be more autonomous overnight.
  • Investor-related research — company lists for warm intros from investors.

7. Dealbreakers

  • Black box behavior with no process visibility
  • Silent failures or silent stalls (the "agent waiting for input" story)
  • Opaque failure modes where he can't understand why it failed
  • Any sense that the agent is eroding his domain expertise without keeping him engaged
  • AI handling personal/creative expression

8. ICP Classification

Strong ICP

Reasoning: Kautuk is the ideal technical ICP. His pain is being the bottleneck — tasks queue up waiting for his attention, not because they're hard. He already delegates 80% of coding to AI and saves 50–60% of his time. He enthusiastically endorses overnight autonomous agents. His trust model is demanding but well-defined (5 visible runs, then full fire-and-forget). The 80% quality threshold means launch agents don't need to be perfect — they need to be fast and transparent.

Why strong: He's already past the adoption threshold for AI delegation. He has concrete tasks he'd delegate tonight (personal branding, research surfacing, investor research). His delegation bottleneck is explaining "how," which is exactly what an agent that learns from examples would solve. He wants queue-drainers, not perfection-seekers — a realistic expectation that a product can meet.
