Launch Agents — User Interview Kit

CE Sprint, Track: Launch Agents · Focus: Level 3 delegation · 2026-03-25

1. Hypotheses

Ten specific, falsifiable beliefs we are testing. Each interview should confirm or kill at least 3–4 of these.

H1: 80% > 100%. Users would rather get an 80% result fast than a 100% result late — especially when they are the bottleneck.

H2: The real competitor is “I’ll do it myself at 2 AM.” Users don’t compare us to other AI tools. They compare us to staying up late and doing the work themselves.

H3: Trust requires a visible trail, not real-time control. A detailed morning briefing (“here’s what I did, here’s what I skipped, here’s what I’m unsure about”) is sufficient to build trust — users don’t need to watch the agent work live.

H4: Level 3 is the sweet spot. Users have tasks that naturally sit at Level 3 (outcome + rough process). They already think in delegation levels even if they don’t use that language.

H5: The first task users would delegate is repetitive-but-judgmental. Think lead enrichment, inbox triage, competitor monitoring — not creative work, not fully mechanical work. The task requires some judgment but the judgment is pattern-matchable.

H6: Identity erosion is the hidden blocker. Some users won’t delegate certain tasks not because they distrust the agent, but because doing the task is part of how they see themselves. Research is the canonical example.

H7: Progressive autonomy works. Users who start by reviewing every agent action will, within 5–10 successful runs, shift to reviewing only exceptions and summaries.

H8: Loss framing beats gain framing. “You spent 3 hours this morning doing what could have been done overnight” is more motivating than “Save 3 hours with our agent.”

H9: Silent failure is worse than visible failure. Users can tolerate an agent that tries and fails (if it tells them). They cannot tolerate an agent that fails silently and they discover the damage later.

H10: The delegation bottleneck is specification, not trust. Some users would happily delegate but struggle to articulate what they want clearly enough. The “documentation paradox” — the people who most need to delegate don’t have time to write the instructions.

2. ICP Persona

The Overwhelmed Operator-Builder

This person runs or co-runs a company (could be a non-technical ops lead or a technical founder). They personally handle 15–40 tasks per week that span domains — sales, ops, product, hiring, content. They are competent enough that delegation always feels like a downgrade: assistants lose context, automations break at the judgment boundary, and freelancers need so much coordination that “I’ll just do it myself” wins by default.

They are already paying $500–3,000/month on some combination of VAs, Zapier, and tool stacks, but remain the glue layer between all of it. Their pain is not any single task — it’s being the bottleneck on everything.

Their dream state is: “I told someone what to do before bed, I woke up, it was done.”

They think in terms of delegation levels, even informally (“this I can hand off, this I can’t”). They would start with Level 3 tasks — clear outcome, rough process, agent uses judgment on the details.

3. Anti-ICP Persona

The Sovereign Thinker

This person is highly capable and delegates only at the extremes: Level 1 (mechanical execution, zero judgment) or Level 5 (full ownership by a trusted expert). They skip everything in between because the middle is where they learn, form opinions, and maintain their edge. Research, analysis, and synthesis are identity-defining activities — delegating them feels like outsourcing their brain.

They may also be someone whose work is inherently non-repeatable (pure creative, high-context strategy) or someone who has already solved the delegation problem with a strong human team. They will say “AI makes me dumber” or “I need to do this myself to stay sharp.”

They may use AI as a tool (copilot, search, drafting) but will resist giving it autonomy. Selling them Launch Agents would require changing their worldview, not their workflow — that is not our job.

4. Interview Questions

Format: Hypothesis Question (expected ICP answer). Keep it conversational. These are meant to be asked in order but skip freely based on flow.

Opening (warm up, 2 min)

Q1. H2 What’s the last work task you did after midnight?

ICP: names a specific task they wish they hadn’t done. Anti-ICP: “I don’t work late” or names something creative/strategic they wanted to do.

Q2. H4 If you had to sort your tasks into “I must do this personally” vs. “someone else could do this if they had enough context” — roughly what percentage falls in each bucket?

ICP: 30–60% could be done by someone else. Anti-ICP: >80% must be done personally.

Delegation behavior (5 min)

Q3. H2 When you last tried to delegate something and it came back wrong — what did you do?

ICP: “Fixed it myself and stopped delegating that task.” Anti-ICP: “I rarely delegate, so this doesn’t come up.”

Q4. H10 What’s harder: deciding WHAT to delegate, or explaining HOW to do it?

H10 true: “explaining how.” H10 false: “deciding what.”

Q5. H4 Think of a task you do regularly that requires some judgment but isn’t creative or strategic. What is it?

ICP: names something concrete — lead qualification, inbox triage, vendor comparison, report generation. Anti-ICP: struggles to name one, or says “everything I do requires real judgment.”

Q6. H5 If an AI could do that task at 80% of your quality, would you let it? Yes or no.

ICP: “Yes, especially if it’s fast.” Anti-ICP: “No, 80% isn’t good enough.”

Q7. H1 Would you rather get that task done at 80% by morning, or 100% by you sometime tomorrow afternoon?

H1 true: “80% by morning.”

Trust and control (5 min)

Q8. H3 Imagine an agent did 5 tasks overnight. You wake up to a summary: what it did, what it skipped, what it wasn’t sure about. On a scale of 1–5, how comfortable is that? (1 = hate it, 5 = love it)

ICP: 3–5. Anti-ICP: 1–2.

Q9. H3 Would you need to watch the agent work in real time, or is a morning debrief enough?

H3 true: “morning debrief is fine.” H3 false: “I’d need to watch.”

Q10. H9 What’s worse: an agent that tries something and fails but tells you immediately, or an agent that silently skips a task because it wasn’t sure?

Most will say silent skipping is worse — confirms H9. Some will say “depends on the task” — useful nuance.

Q11. H7 If the agent got it right 5 times in a row, would you stop checking every detail and just skim the summary?

H7 true: “Probably yes.” Anti-ICP: “No, I’d always check.”

Q12. H9 Have you ever been burned by an automation or tool failing silently? What happened?

Rich story = strong signal for H9. No story = weaker signal but still useful.

Identity and resistance (3 min)

Q13. H6 Is there a type of work you’d refuse to delegate even if the AI could do it perfectly? What is it, and why?

H6 true: names something and the reason is identity/learning, not quality. “Research makes me smarter.” “Writing is how I think.”

Q14. H6 If an AI did your research for you every day for a month, would you feel sharper or duller at the end?

H6 true: “Duller.” ICP who is not anti-ICP: “Sharper, because I’d have more time for the hard stuff.”

Value and urgency (3 min)

Q15. H8 Think about this morning. How many hours did you spend on tasks that could have been done overnight if someone reliable had handled them?

ICP: names a number, usually 1–4 hours. Anti-ICP: “None, really.”

Q16. H8 If I told you “you lost 2 hours this morning doing work that could have been done while you slept” — does that sting, or does it feel irrelevant?

H8 true: “That stings.” Anti-ICP: “I don’t think of it that way.”

Q17. H1 H2 What would you delegate tonight if you could? Name one task.

ICP: answers immediately with something specific. Anti-ICP: hesitates or says “nothing comes to mind.”

Surprise me (2 min)

Q18. What’s the dumbest thing you spend time on every week?

Open-ended. Looking for task candidates and emotional valence.

Q19. If this product existed and worked perfectly, what’s the first thing that could go wrong that would make you stop using it?

Trust signal. Looking for: “sent something wrong on my behalf,” “made a bad decision I couldn’t undo,” “I didn’t know what it did.”

Q20. Anything else about how you work that you think I should know?

Catch-all. Sometimes the best insight comes here.

Classification

Strong ICP: 7–10 ICP signals, 0–1 anti-ICP signals. Prioritize for beta.

Moderate ICP: 4–6 ICP signals, 0–2 anti-ICP signals. Viable user, may need onboarding nudges.

Mixed/Unclear: 3–5 ICP signals, 3+ anti-ICP signals. Interesting for research, not for beta.

Anti-ICP: 0–3 ICP signals, 4+ anti-ICP signals. Thank them, learn from the resistance, move on.

Interview Logistics

  • Duration: 15–20 minutes max. Bite-sized questions keep it tight.
  • Recording: Ask permission. If no, take notes on the scoring grid in real time.
  • Stance: Curious, not selling. We are testing hypotheses, not pitching a product.
  • Key rule: If someone is clearly anti-ICP by Q5, skip to Q13–Q14 (identity questions) — that’s where the most useful anti-ICP insight lives. Don’t waste their time on trust/control questions that won’t apply.
  • After each interview: Fill the scoring grid and hypothesis tracker immediately. Takes 2 minutes. Don’t batch this — you’ll forget nuance.
