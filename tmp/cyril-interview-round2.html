1. The 70/30 Split

Delegation Split

30% personal
70% delegatable

70% of Cyril's tasks are delegatable — repeatable process work with clear inputs and outputs. 30% he wants to do personally.

The 70%: payroll, research curation, HR logistics, calendar management.

The 30%: the things that require his judgment, relationships, and context that can't be written down.

This is the most delegation-friendly split on the team (Deepanshi: 10%, Sagar: 20%, Ritam: 60% after AI offloading). And critically, Cyril's 70% is almost entirely process work — not creative or research tasks, but step-by-step procedures with clear completion criteria.

2. Payroll: The Perfect Delegation Case Study

Cyril walked through his entire payroll process unprompted. Ten steps, fully manual, zero judgment required. This is the single best example of an agent-ready task from any interview.

The 10-Step Process

  1. Determine who's in his purview (not core team: Ritam, Deepanshi, Sabesh, etc.)
  2. Create spreadsheet, check days worked and leave taken
  3. Check contracts for compensation details
  4. Check Slack DMs for reimbursement claims
  5. Check emails for bonuses or adjustments
  6. Calculate total INR tally per person
  7. Go to each person's bank's forex page
  8. Get the telegraphic transfer rate for USD to INR that day
  9. Divide INR by forex rate to get USD amount
  10. Put into Mercury, create transfer, get approval

Why this matters: Pure process. Zero judgment. Currently 100% manual. Every step has a clear input, a clear output, and a verifiable completion state. Steps 1-9 are fully automatable. Step 10 (Mercury transfer + approval) is the one irreversible action that needs a human in the loop. This is the textbook agent task.

3. Memory Failure = Trust Death

This is the single most important product insight from all Round 2 interviews.

The Claude Story

Cyril tried Claude for personal branding and content planning:

  • Taught it his voice — lowercase, profanity, personal style
  • Fed it tweet dumps, algorithm knowledge, brand strategy
  • Built up a full content plan over a session
  • Next session: Claude forgot everything. Talked in proper English. Lost the plan entirely.

"If you don't have a mechanism to consume feedback and remember it, there's no point."

He gave up. He hasn't used it since.

The #1 Product Signal: Memory isn't a feature. It's the feature. An agent that forgets your teaching is worse than no agent at all, because you've already invested the time to teach it. Cyril didn't gradually lose trust. He lost it in one session. The moment the agent spoke in proper English instead of his voice, it wasn't his agent anymore. It was a stranger.

4. Test Mode vs. Production Mode

Cyril has an explicitly engineering-oriented mental model for how he evaluates agents.

"I have a very engineer-oriented mind — you do certain things for me in test mode, then I put you in production mode."

Test Mode Production Mode
Patience High. Willing to teach, iterate, give feedback. Low. Errors are not tolerable.
Expectation "Take your time, do it right the first time." "You've been trained. Perform."
Failure tolerance Expected and welcomed. Learning is the point. Minimal. "If you f*** up in production mode — there's only so much threshold I have."

Design implication: The onboarding phase IS the test mode. Users need to feel like they're teaching the agent, with visible learning. Then when they flip to "production" — trusting it with real tasks — the agent must perform at the level it demonstrated during testing. Any regression from test-mode quality to production-mode quality is a betrayal.

5. Specificity > Generality

"If an agent comes up and says 'I can do everything' — give me an empty field? Your job is to figure out what you can do best."

Cyril wants agents that declare specific capabilities, not generalist blank canvases.

  • Analogy: You hire finance people for finance, not generalists. You don't hire someone and say "figure out what you're good at."
  • Exception: Startups hire generalists because the company itself is figuring out what it does.
  • But for agents: "I would like my agent to function as if they were an enterprise" — specific, reliable, process-driven.

This directly contradicts the "one agent for everything" vision that most AI companies sell. Cyril would rather have 5 agents that each do one thing well than 1 agent that claims to do everything and does nothing reliably.

6. 80% Serial >> 80% Random (The Lego Piece Rule)

Two ways an agent can complete 8 of 10 steps:

Pattern What it looks like User experience
Steps 1-8 done sequentially Agent does the first 80%, stops at a clear point, hands off to user for steps 9-10. "I am just the lego piece that connects two arms. I'm fine."
Random 8 of 10 done Agent does steps 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9, 10. User must figure out which 2 are missing and fill gaps. "I will kill someone."

The Lego Piece Rule: The user should be a known insertion point in a sequential process, not a detective figuring out what's missing. Design agent handoffs as clean breaks in a pipeline, never as gap-filling exercises.

7. Reversibility as Trust Framework

Maps directly to Bezos's two-way/one-way door framework. Cyril applies it instinctively.

"If it's reversible, figure out, find out. If it's irreversible — I would love to be in the loop."

Reversible (two-way door) Irreversible (one-way door)
Calendar events Money transfers
Research drafts Contracts
Content drafts External communications
"Do whatever. Figure it out." → ← "I must be in the room."

Design implication: Every task the agent handles should be classified as reversible or irreversible. Reversible tasks get full autonomy. Irreversible tasks get a mandatory approval gate. The agent should know the difference and never ask for permission on reversible actions or skip permission on irreversible ones.

8. Silent Failure is the Cardinal Sin

Cyril laid out a clear hierarchy of failure modes, ranked from best to worst.

Rank Failure Mode Verdict
1. BEST Does it well. Obviously ideal.
2. GOOD Fails but notifies immediately. Acceptable. Agent shows accountability.
3. OK Fails, surfaces in user space for async review. Tolerable. At least it's discoverable.
4. FIRED Fails silently. Doesn't tell you. "People get fired over that — as employees."

"Tell me you didn't do it."

"AI should take accountability."

Non-negotiable: Silent failure detection is table stakes. An agent that fails and doesn't report it is worse than an agent that never attempted the task. The gap between rank 3 (async failure surfacing) and rank 4 (silent failure) is the gap between "tolerable" and "fired." There is no product without solving this.

9. Trust Building: Gradual + Measurable

The 5-in-a-Row Rule

5 correct completions in a row and Cyril stops checking details. Trust is earned through consistent, sequential success.

Research Curation Test

For research tasks, the agent must capture everything Cyril already knows plus surface extras. His evaluation framework:

"If it gives me the 5 things I know + 3 I don't know + 2 bullshit = highly appreciated."

"If it gives me 3 I know + 3 I don't know + 2 bullshit = I still rely on myself for the missing 2."

Missing known items = trust broken. Extra noise = tolerable. The baseline is completeness of what the user already knows, not novelty.

Payroll Trust Threshold

For high-stakes process work like payroll: approximately 3 months of correct execution before he'd stop manually verifying every step.

The trust math: Trust is a ramp, not a switch. 5 successes for low-stakes tasks, ~3 months for high-stakes process work. Missing something the user already knows is worse than including noise they don't need. Design the trust gradient accordingly: start with low-stakes tasks, build the streak, graduate to higher-stakes work.

10. Force Multiplier, Not Replacement

Cyril wants to stay in the value chain. The ideal model: AI surfaces information, he forms inferences, he communicates.

"If it completely replaces me, I would probably feel dumber. But that's a me problem, not an AI problem."

Research specifically should be a dialogue, not a delegation. The agent gathers, filters, and presents. Cyril synthesizes and decides.

This places Cyril between Deepanshi ("process IS the learning, don't take it from me") and Ritam ("delegate and never think about it"). Cyril wants the grunt work done but wants to remain the decision-maker and the communicator. The agent is the back office; he's the front office.

11. Tonight's Delegation Wishlist

If an agent were ready tonight, here's what Cyril would hand it, in priority order:

  1. DELEGATE Competitor research. Daily AI landscape monitoring with relevance filtering. What's new, what matters to CE, what's noise.
  2. DELEGATE Payroll automation. The full 10-step process above, with human approval at step 10.
  3. DELEGATE HR bot. Birthday reminders (2 days advance), gift ordering based on office check-in presence, delivery scheduling.
  4. DELEGATE Weekly employee sentiment surveys. Private, secure, aggregated for him to review.
  5. MIXED Capacity planning. Pull data from Linear + morale data, surface recommendations. He decides.
  6. DELEGATE Calendar events from documents. Sprint milestones in docs should automatically become calendar invites.
  7. DELEGATE Contract template filling and sending. Fill templates from structured data, send for review.

Notice the pattern: 6 of 7 are pure process automation. Only capacity planning involves synthesis. Cyril's delegation instinct is overwhelmingly toward mechanical tasks, not cognitive ones.

12. The Dealbreaker Question

"What fuckup would make you stop using this product?"

  1. Irreversible action without permission. The one-way door opened without asking.
  2. Not flagging blockers. Encountering an obstacle and plowing through or ignoring it.
  3. Unjustified assumptions. "Explain your assumptions. If your assumption is on shaking ground and you can't justify it..."
  4. Doing things without adequate explanation. Action without reasoning = unacceptable.

The common thread: transparency. Every dealbreaker is about the agent acting without the user understanding why. Not about the agent being wrong — about the agent being opaque.

13. Dumbest Time Sink

20 minutes across 3 e-commerce apps looking for Diet Coke. Couldn't find it. Went out and bought it in person.

The kind of task that feels too trivial to delegate but costs real time. A reminder that "delegatable" isn't always about importance — sometimes it's about friction.

14. Product Implications

What Cyril's interview means for what we build.

Six Product Decisions from This Interview

  1. Memory is THE feature. Cyril literally quit Claude over memory failure. An agent that forgets your teaching is worse than no agent. Persistent memory across sessions is non-negotiable.
  2. Capability declaration > empty canvas. Don't give users a blank prompt. Tell them what the agent can do. "I handle payroll, calendar, and research curation" beats "How can I help you today?"
  3. Sequential completeness with clear handoff points. The Lego Piece Rule. Agent does steps 1-8, hands off at a clean break. Never does a random subset and asks the user to figure out what's missing.
  4. Reversibility-aware task classification. Every action the agent can take must be tagged as reversible or irreversible. Reversible = autonomous. Irreversible = approval gate. The agent must know the difference without being told each time.
  5. Silent failure detection is table stakes. If the agent fails and doesn't tell you, the product is broken. Period. This is rank 4 in Cyril's hierarchy. "People get fired over that."
  6. Trust is a ramp, not a switch. Design for progressive autonomy. Start with research (low stakes, easy to verify). Graduate to process automation (high stakes, longer trust runway). 5 successes for low-stakes. 3 months for payroll-grade trust.
