How Might We — 12 Statements

Sprint Day 2 — Gate 2 deliverable (2.4) · Derived from competitor audit, user interviews, and research

All 12 HMW Statements

HMW #1 Top Pick #1
How might we let users delegate tasks that require judgment without forcing them to write detailed instructions?
Source: H10 (specification bottleneck), Deepanshi interview (won't delegate research), Cyril's L3 sweet spot
Why this is #1: This IS the specification bottleneck. Every competitor either requires a detailed workflow (Lindy, Notion) or fails at ambiguity (Devin, Manus). Solving this = solving Level 3.
HMW #2 Top Pick #2
How might we make the agent's work visible enough to build trust, without requiring the user to watch it work in real time?
Source: H3 (visible trail > real-time control), competitor failure pattern #2 (silent failure), Kautuk's "80% done" threshold
Why this is #2: Trust is THE adoption barrier. The audit trail / morning briefing is Felix's structural advantage over every competitor that fails silently.
HMW #3
How might we price the product so users never feel anxious about experimenting?
Source: Competitor pattern #1 (credit anxiety kills experimentation — Lindy, Manus, Notion), Athena's flat-rate model works psychologically
HMW #4
How might we handle failure loudly enough that users trust us, without overwhelming them with noise?
Source: H9 (silent failure is worse than visible failure), Cursor's silent code reversion, Manus empty ZIPs
HMW #5 Top Pick #3
How might we bridge the gap between “$0 chatbots” and “$3,000/month human EAs” for non-technical professionals?
Source: Market gap analysis — the empty quadrant (mid-cost, high-autonomy, judgment-based)
Why this is #3: This is the market positioning. Every other HMW is a feature; this one is the business.
HMW #6
How might we help users start with simple delegation and naturally progress to trusting the agent with harder tasks?
Source: H7 (progressive autonomy), Cyril's Level 1–5 framework (users start at L1 and move up)
HMW #7
How might we frame overnight agent work as “what you would have stayed up doing” rather than “a new AI feature”?
Source: H2 (real competitor is "I'll do it myself at 2 AM"), H8 (loss framing > gain framing)
HMW #8
How might we let users delegate across different domains (research, ops, content) without building a separate workflow for each?
Source: Ritam's L1 critique of hardcoded pipelines, Lindy/Notion = workflow builders not agents, Felix's toolbox approach
HMW #9
How might we preserve user identity in areas where doing the work = being themselves, while still saving them time elsewhere?
Source: H6 (identity erosion), Deepanshi won't delegate research ("makes me smarter"), anti-ICP persona
HMW #10
How might we make the agent's judgment traceable so users can calibrate trust over time?
Source: Competitor pattern #5 (no recourse when things go wrong), H3 (audit trail), Cyril's 2D model (task complexity × trust)
HMW #11
How might we deliver “I told someone, I came back, it was done” at a price point 10–50x cheaper than a human EA?
Source: Athena = gold standard UX at $36K/year, all three interviewees described this dream state
HMW #12
How might we avoid the demo-to-reality gap that killed trust for Devin, Rabbit R1, and Humane AI Pin?
Source: Competitor pattern #3 (demo vs reality), Devin HN backlash, Rabbit 95% abandonment

The Gap Statement

Primary — Section 2.2

No product today lets a non-technical professional delegate a multi-hour, judgment-requiring task to an AI agent that works asynchronously, uses its own judgment on process decisions, and delivers a completed result with a clear audit trail — at a price point between $0 (chatbots) and $3,000/month (human EAs).

Alternative — shorter

The gap between “I'll just do it myself at 2 AM” and “I told someone, I came back, it was done” has no affordable AI solution.
