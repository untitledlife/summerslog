Interview Insights: Sagar

iOS Developer, Conscious Engines — Launch Agents interview, 25 March 2026

1. Profile

Role: iOS developer at Conscious Engines. Hands-on engineer building UI with SwiftUI. Currently also doing backend/research work for the sprint.

Context: Interviewed by Ritam using the standard Launch Agents interview kit (same questions as all other interviewees: last task after midnight, delegation buckets, failure tolerance, overnight agents, trust thresholds, dealbreakers).

Interview date: March 25, 2026 (18:10 recording).

2. Delegation Philosophy

Delegation ratio: ~80/20 (80% personal, 20% delegatable). Sagar does not delegate much in his iOS role but is very open to delegation for research and backend tasks.

“Depends on my role — previously on the iOS role, I don’t think there is something which AI can do which I would delegate. But in the backend role I would want it to do a differentiation between Deepgram and AssemblyAI and come up with pros and cons of both.”

His delegation stance is role-dependent and task-categorical. He has a clear mental map of what is delegatable (research, code review, prototyping) and what is not (production merges, personal communication). Unlike some other interviewees, he does not struggle with deciding what to delegate — he struggles with the overhead of explaining it:

“If I’m delegating, why do I want to give context?”

He wants Level 4 autonomy: give minimal context, agent figures out the approach. Not Level 3 (specify the rough process).

“I will give a very small quantity [of context]. This is the transcription, but go and figure out shit on your own.”

3. Key Insights

  1. Code review is his #1 engineering delegation wish. He wants deep, automated PR review that runs whenever he pushes: finding everything that can go wrong, fixing what it can, and flagging what it cannot.

    “I would want someone to really, really go in and figure out everything that can go wrong [in my PR]. It should be automated whenever I do changes.”

  2. Research is the overnight delegation sweet spot. He has many research tasks accumulating and wants 2–3 deep research tasks delegated nightly, each producing a concise report with a recommendation.

    “I have so much to research today. If I by 9 PM go and okay do this research, create these websites, create very small reports, I would love it by morning.”

  3. Failures should be documented in the report, not sent as interruptions. He draws a clear line: no real-time error banners, no constant notifications. Batch failures into the morning report. The agent should work around what it cannot do and flag it inline.

    “I would not want error banners. I would want the report. It says okay I was unable to find the [API] but I have done rest of the things. I have left that commented out.”

  4. Morning brief is the ideal interaction model. Fire-and-forget at night, summary in the morning. He does not want to monitor the agent working.

    “If it does it at night and in morning I get okay, I have done this, I have not done this — that would be very good.”

  5. Concise output is non-negotiable. He explicitly hates long-form text and wants reports limited to 1–2 pages. Voice delivery is preferred over reading.

    “I don’t like reading. I hate reading things. If I get a report it should be one page at most 2 pages. Be vocal.”

  6. Research + prototype is the killer combo. Not just research — he wants the agent to research a topic and then build a working prototype of the finding.

    “Research karo, uska prototype banao — not touching deployed stuff, just how your Summer is doing things.”

  7. Already trusts AI for research. Unlike other interviewees who need trust to build over time, Sagar already trusts ChatGPT for research output. His trust barrier is task-categorical, not progressive.

    “If it’s research, I’m trusting ChatGPT.”

4. Pain Points

  • Too many research tasks, not enough time. Has accumulated multiple research threads (tool comparisons, open-source evaluations) that he cannot get to during working hours. Estimates 2–3 deep research tasks could be offloaded nightly.
  • Manual multi-step workflow for content generation. Currently: take transcript, go to ChatGPT, get press release, copy to doc, then build website, then deploy. Wants this collapsed into one delegation.
  • Context-giving overhead. Finds the process of explaining tasks burdensome. Wants to give minimal input and have the agent figure out the rest.
  • Long text output is unusable for him. Deep research tools produce verbose reports he will not read. Wants concise (1–2 page) or voice-based delivery.

5. Trust Model

Dimension Sagar’s Approach
Trust building Results-based and task-categorical. Already trusts AI for research. Very high bar for production code actions.
Trust threshold Low for research (already trusts ChatGPT). Effectively infinite for merging code — won’t allow it regardless of track record.
Failure handling Graceful degradation: agent should work around failures, comment out what it cannot do, and include everything in the morning report.
Agent behavior Fire-and-forget at night, morning report. No real-time monitoring needed for research tasks.
Trust transition Categorical, not progressive. Research is trusted. Production code is not. No amount of successful runs changes the production boundary.

6. What He’d Delegate Tonight

  • 2–3 deep research tasks — tool comparisons, framework evaluations, competitive analysis. Each producing a concise report with a recommendation and reasoning.
  • Automated code review — deep PR review that finds everything that can go wrong, fixes what it can, and flags the rest as comments.
  • Prototype scaffolding — research a topic, then build a working prototype. Sandbox only, not touching production.
  • Website/content generation — take a transcript, generate press release, build and deploy a simple website.

7. Dealbreakers

  • Merging code or taking production actions. “I wouldn’t give it full control because I don’t own the product.” Autonomous merges are an absolute no.
  • Talking to people on his behalf. Personal messages (girlfriend, friends) and professional communications are off-limits. Work-related Slack updates are a maybe for the future.
  • Verbose output. Long reports get deleted. If the agent produces walls of text, he stops using it.
  • Frequent real-time error interruptions. Error banners and constant failure notifications break the fire-and-forget model.

8. ICP Classification

Strong ICP

Reasoning: Sagar is a clear match for the core Launch Agents use case. He actively wants overnight research delegation (2–3 tasks per night), already trusts AI for research output, prefers fire-and-forget with morning summary, is willing to pay $30/month, and has concrete accumulated pain (too many research tasks, not enough time). His boundaries (no production merges, no personal communication) are standard across all interviewees and do not conflict with the v1 product scope.

Segment: IC engineer with research backlog. Similar to Sahil in role but much higher delegation willingness and lower trust threshold for research tasks.
