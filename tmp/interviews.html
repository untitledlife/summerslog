Internal Interviews & ICP Analysis

First-party research from inside the team — March 24, 2026

The Delegation Spectrum

From Cyril's mental model (validated across all three interviews):

  • Level 1: You give the outcome AND the exact process. The person follows steps. → This is automation. Zapier handles it.
  • Level 2: You give the outcome and most of the process, person fills small gaps.
  • Level 3: You give the outcome, you have a rough idea of the process, the person figures it out and adds judgment. → This is the sweet spot for launch agents. Nothing fills this gap today.
  • Level 4: You give the outcome, the person designs the process entirely.
  • Level 5: You give the outcome, the person figures out the problem itself. → This needs a human (or a very trusted senior).

Key insight: Level 3 is where every current solution fails. Automation can't judge. Humans lose context. Current AI agents hallucinate or burn credits. The person who occupies Level 3 today is... the founder, at 2 AM.

Interview 1: Deepanshi (Product & Design Lead)

Profile

Product manager who also handles interior decoration, design management, and vendor coordination. Extreme context-switching across 4+ domains daily. Self-described: "I'd rather put 5 more minutes and do it perfectly than deal with errors and trials."

Current Workflow

Whiteboard/meeting → Claude (central brain, everything funnels through it) → Notion (storage, docs, re-reading) → specialized tools (Chronicle for decks, Figma, Midjourney, ChatGPT images).

  • Uses ChatGPT voice mode for brainstorming, then asks it to generate Claude prompts
  • Claude is the single hub: all thinking, structuring, and content creation starts there
  • Pays ~₹1,500/month across tools (Chronicle alone for presentations)
  • Typical flow: photo of whiteboard → dump into Claude with framework prompt → get structured doc → copy to Notion → add visual proof (photos of diagrams) → distribute

What She'd Delegate

YES Low-stakes "has to be done" tasks (comms, follow-ups, unblocking juniors)

YES Presentation creation (when she knows exactly what she wants)

YES User interview prep (script generation, question lists with context)

YES Boilerplate generation with context

NO Research ("it makes me smarter — I don't want AI doing my learning for me")

NO User interviews themselves (need empathy, presence, follow-up instincts)

NO High-judgment product decisions

NO Personal life tasks ("I don't want to use AI for my personal life")

#1 Pain: Memory

"If Claude had memory... instead of asking 'how was your work, do you use any productivity tool?' it should know: 'How is it working with Felix? Are you still using it? Do you do real tasks?'"

She manually copies context from Notion back into Claude before every session. The tool doesn't remember her, her team, or previous conversations. She wants continuity, not just capability.

Delegation Philosophy

"I'd rather do it myself. Teaching someone is time-consuming and error-prone. I'll put 5 more minutes and do it perfectly."

Trust threshold is very high. She's a Level 1 or Level 5 delegator, nothing in between. Either give exact instructions and expect perfect execution, or don't bother. This means launch agents for Deepanshi must prove reliability on trivial tasks before she'll trust them with anything meaningful.

Real-Time Proof of Pain

Got a vendor phone call mid-interview about furniture advance payments (₹22,000, raw materials, delivery schedule). Had to switch from product manager to interior designer to user interviewee in 90 seconds. Said afterward: "See, this is the problem. I have to context-switch from product manager to interior designer to designer, and back."

Interview 2: Cyril (Ops Lead) — Summary

Profile

Handles HR, ops, engineering setup (CMS), compliance, partnerships with AI labs, procurement, accountability partner, payroll, hiring, leave policies, and "everything in between." His pain is breadth, not depth.

Delegation Model

Created the Level 1-5 delegation spectrum (see above). His ideal: give an outcome and rough direction (Level 3), get back a completed task with judgment applied. Currently, he IS the coordination layer — the person who holds context across every domain.

Launch Agent Fit

Perfect non-technical ICP. His tasks are high-breadth, medium-depth, and mostly about coordination and follow-through. Many of his tasks are Level 2-3: the outcome is clear, the process is roughly known, but someone needs to apply context and judgment at each step.

Interview 3: Kautuk (Founder/CEO) — Summary

Profile

Founder, also handles backend. The bottleneck. Things wait on him because nobody else has enough context to move them forward.

The 80% Threshold

"If all his tasks were done at least 80%, he'd be happy."

From PR reviews to POC features. He takes action on urgency — urgency defined as "he's the blocker." The queue problem: tasks pile up not because they're hard, but because they're waiting for his attention.

Launch Agent Fit

Technical ICP. His pain is being the bottleneck, not breadth. Launch agents as queue-drainers: take tasks from 0% to 80%, so he only needs to review and ship. Not perfection-seekers — unblock-ers.

Cross-Interview Patterns

What All Three Share

  1. Context is the #1 problem. Every interviewee mentioned context loss as the primary barrier to delegation. Tools don't remember. People leave. Onboarding takes too long.
  2. "I'll just do it myself" is the default coping mechanism — not because they want to, but because the cost of bad delegation is higher than the cost of doing it themselves.
  3. Trust is earned, not given. Nobody said "I'd immediately trust an AI agent with important work." The trust curve is real and steep.
  4. They already use AI — heavily. But as fragmented tools, not as agents. Claude, ChatGPT, Notion AI, Midjourney. The gap isn't "will they use AI?" but "will they let AI act autonomously?"

The Delegation Split

Person Delegation Style Sweet Spot
Deepanshi Level 1 or 5, nothing between Boilerplate + prep with context
Cyril Level 2-3, breadth-focused Coordination + follow-through
Kautuk Level 3-4, bottleneck-focused Queue-draining to 80%

Non-ICP: Who This Is NOT For

  • Enterprise teams with dedicated ops people — they already have humans for coordination
  • Developers who'd rather script it themselves — they want APIs, not agents
  • Users with simple single-step tasks — just use chat, no need for long-horizon
  • Highly regulated workflows where AI autonomy is a compliance issue (healthcare decisions, legal filings)
  • Anyone not already drowning in "I am the glue" — if they're not feeling the pain, they won't trust an agent to swim for them

Anti-Use Cases: Where Launch Agents Should NOT Go

  • Irreversible high-stakes actions (sending money, publishing contracts, deleting data) — at minimum, not without explicit human gates
  • Real-time back-and-forth (negotiation, live customer support, user interviews)
  • Tasks with no clear success criteria ("make my business better")
  • Emotional/relational work (apologies, difficult conversations, HR)
  • Simple one-off lookups (overkill — just use chat)
  • Tasks where cost of failure > cost of doing it manually

The sharp filter: If the task doesn't span hours, doesn't require judgment at handoffs, or doesn't currently keep someone up at night — launch agents are the wrong tool.
