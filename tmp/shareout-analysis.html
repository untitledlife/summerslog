Track 2 — Morning Brief / Discovery

Deepanshi + Sabesh

Problem

Overcommitted people waste time filtering notifications instead of focusing on high-impact work.

Key Pivot

Competition isn’t apps (Sunflower, etc.) — it’s Cyril, the human proxy. The relationship is the product, not the features.

Identity Erosion Insight

The real cost isn’t time, it’s becoming unreliable.

“Help me be the person I said I would be.”

Refined ICP

Mid-level manager or founder who is the single point of failure. Highly capable, operating behind capacity. Their commitments determine other people’s day.

Risk Assumption

“The user is ready to confront their unreliability” — overcommitted people are protective of their self-image.

Kautuk’s Feedback

Problem statement was confusing, feature-focused (“notifications”), structure unclear. Pushed them to clarify what signal they’re extracting from noise. Landed on: “helping me tell me the top 3 things” = prioritized signal extraction.

Track 4 — Meeting Notes

Sagar

Problem

Meetings poorly prepared, poorly followed up, causing time loss and context loss.

Three Phases

  • Pre-meet: No context going in
  • During-meet: Can’t capture live
  • Post-meet: No actionable follow-up

Current Workarounds

  • Deepanshi: Uses a Claude pipeline (transcript → Claude → follow-up questions) — but no memory across sessions
  • Kautuk: Uses WhisperFlow daily journal dump — gets lost

Risk

What if the pre-meet report exists but the user doesn’t use it? Or worse, it’s wrong?

Kautuk’s Feedback

Too many features. Narrow to IRL meetings. Press release format interesting but scope needs tightening.

Interesting Detail

Deepanshi’s interview workflow: takes interview transcripts → Claude → “give me more questions” → progressive interview depth. Memory is the missing piece.

Kautuk’s Track — Share with Felix / Saved Items

Problem

“Curious people save things they genuinely want to return to, but later never arrives.”

Deep Psychology

Why do we make commitments we can’t keep? Instant gratification — acknowledging work feels like doing work. Same applies to saving content.

Key Insight: Pseudo-Completion

The act of saving relieves the anxiety of “I might miss this.” Saving = consuming psychologically. The bookmark graveyard grows.

9 Hypotheses Tested (User Interviews)

  • INVALIDATED: Format is the barrier (no — people don’t return to images either)
  • MAYBE: Idle moments = consumption windows (no — idle time is for idle stuff)
  • MAYBE: Opening an app = free time signal (no — people prefer feed over saved)
  • YES: People will NOT initiate retrieval themselves (strong)
  • YES: Completion is reward (checking off = self-validation)
  • YES: “Later” container must be created for people (they can’t find their own “later”)
  • STRONG YES: Context is universal trigger (contextual surfacing >> random surfacing)
  • Active vs passive content distinction matters

Two Personas

  • Learner: Wants to consume, never remembers to (Ritam)
  • Curator: Saves references, will search (Sagar)

Risk Assumption

“People actually want to consume what they save” — maybe they don’t. Saving for the act of saving = non-ICP (performative organizers).

Track 5 — Launch Agents

Ritam + Cyril

ICP

Founders, operators who have many tasks in breadth, competent but no bandwidth.

Cyril’s Delegation Framework

Task = outcome + process.

  • Level 1: Specify both outcome and process (recipe → automation)
  • Level 3: Outcome + rough process + judgment (sweet spot for current AI)
  • Level 5: Outcome only, figure out the rest (north star, requires deep trust)

Key Point

Cyril operates at Level 5 because of 6 months of trust-building with Kautuk. New agents start at Level 3.

Risk Assumptions

  1. User trusts platform for read AND write permissions (write = execution = high bar)
  2. Product provides enough value to become sticky

Discussion: Personal vs Work

Deepanshi’s question: Where do founders want to save time — personal or work?

  • Kautuk: Personal (cleaning house, managing maid — “if AI ran my house while I’m not home”)
  • Insight: Execution in the digital realm is feasible now; physical world still needs trust infrastructure

Cyril’s Accountability Argument

Level 5 tasks transfer trust AND accountability. “If I can’t put an AI on the stand in court, I can’t give it Level 5.” Level 3–4 is realistic near-term.

Kautuk: Building agents for Level 5 is the north star, not the today product.

Cross-Track Themes

1. Trust Is the Core Currency

Every track hits this. Trust in the app, trust in the brief, trust in the agent.

2. Human Proxy as Competition

Track 2’s biggest insight. Not competing with apps — competing with Cyril/VA relationships.

3. Memory Is Everyone’s Pain

Deepanshi’s Claude workflow breaks without it. Kautuk’s WhisperFlow gets lost. Saved items become graveyards.

4. Level 3 Delegation Is the Realistic Target

Outcome + rough direction + judgment. Not Level 1 (automation) or Level 5 (full autonomy).

5. Identity and Psychology Matter

Identity erosion (Track 2), pseudo-completion (Kautuk), accountability transfer (Cyril). This isn’t a features problem.

Kautuk’s Overall Feedback Pattern

He consistently pushed teams to:

  • Clarify the problem statement (not solution-first)
  • Validate with actual users (not assumptions)
  • Narrow scope drastically
  • Distinguish between feature-level and relationship-level solutions

What This Means for Track 5 Prototype

Ritam’s presentation was solid on the framework (Cyril’s Level 1–5 is very clear) but lighter on specific problem validation compared to Kautuk’s deep psychology work. For the prototype, the framework is the strength — Level 3 delegation is the sweet spot, and the demo should show an agent operating at exactly that level: “here’s the outcome I want, here’s roughly how, use your judgment.”

The “Competitor Overnight Brief” demo scenario maps perfectly to Level 3: outcome (know what competitors changed), rough process (check these pages), judgment (interpret what it means, flag uncertainty).
