Interview Insights: Ritam

AI/Research, Conscious Engines — Self-interview

1. Profile

Role: AI/Research at Conscious Engines. Technical builder who uses AI as core infrastructure for coding and research.

Context: Self-interview conducted as part of the CE sprint research exercise. Plays both interviewer and interviewee using pre-written structured questions about delegation, task management, and AI assistance.

Interview date: March 25, 2026 (~16 min, self-administered).

2. Delegation Philosophy

Delegation ratio: 80/20 (inverted). 80% of his work must be done personally (with AI help). 20% is delegatable to other people.

Already delegating most coding to AI — AI saves him 50–60% of his coding time. What remains personally: research ideation, product vision, strategic judgment, creative/personal expression.

Delegation failure pattern: When someone fails a delegated task, Ritam takes it back and does it himself. This is a recognized personality trait, not a strategy. He does not iterate with the person who failed.

"If I delegate some task to someone else and they f*** up. I usually do that myself, which is sort of my nature."

The context transfer problem: Explaining "how" is tedious. He knows what needs doing but articulating steps for others is draining. This is a key friction point for delegation.

"Explaining how to do it is difficult for me."

3. Key Insights

  1. Already past the AI adoption threshold. AI is infrastructure, not a tool being evaluated. 50–60% of coding time saved.

    "For most of my coding tasks I delegate to AI. So I am already doing there."

  2. Enthusiastic about overnight autonomous agents. The idea of waking up to 5 completed tasks with a summary is the killer use case.

    "Imagine your agent did 5 tasks overnight, and you woke up with a summary, what it did... how comfortable is that? I love it."

  3. Control anxiety he is actively managing. Discomfort with not knowing what is happening, but training himself out of it.

    "There's kind of uncomfortable if I don't know what is happening. But I'm getting used to that feeling, but I think I am already enough used to it."

  4. Trust through process visibility. Requires 5 successful runs with visible process before he stops checking details. Trust is in the workflow, not just the outcome.

    "I need to know the steps that have gone through in the first 5 times... not just did it 5 times."

  5. Binary trust handoff. Once trust is established, goes fully hands-off — "delegate and never think about it." No gradual ramp-down.

    "If I'm confident enough that the AI agent can do the task I can just delegate that task and never think about it."

  6. Burned by silent failures. Claude agent waiting for input while he thought it was working. No notification. Significant time wasted.

    "I thought my agent is working on something. But it was actually waiting for my input. And I wasted a lot of my time there."

  7. Nuanced view on research delegation. If AI surfaces research and keeps him accountable to read it, he feels sharper. If he stops reading, he won't feel dumber but won't grow either.

    "AI is actually surfacing information that I would not have otherwise read."

  8. User interviews valued as learning. Could have saved 2–3 hours by delegating, but values them as a learning process.

    "I feel this user interview part of this sprint is kind of like a learning process for me."

  9. Personal/creative tasks are a hard boundary. Will not delegate messages, drawings, or anything with personal expression.

    "Something that I don't want AI to do is... message my girlfriend on my behalf. Draw things for someone... mostly creative things."

  10. Black box failures are a dealbreaker. If he cannot see the workflow and the product fails, he will stop using it. Transparency is non-negotiable.

    "If it reliably fails and then will black box, then any failure rate is actually very concerning."

4. Pain Points

  • Silent failures / stuck processes. The worst experience: thinking an agent was working when it was waiting for input. No notification.
  • Explaining "how" is tedious. The bottleneck is articulating steps, not identifying what to delegate.
  • Lack of visibility into agent work. Creates anxiety. He's training himself out of it, but it remains a real barrier.
  • Meetings feel like wasted time. Explicitly called out as unproductive.
  • Personal brand procrastination. Knows he should build his brand, activation energy too high.
  • Instagram scrolling as time sink. Self-aware about it.
  • Black box products. If he cannot see the workflow and it fails, he will abandon it.

5. Trust Model

Dimension How Ritam Builds/Loses Trust
Building trust 5 successful runs with visible process. Must see the workflow, not just outcomes.
Trust transition Binary. High oversight → full fire-and-forget. Once confident, delegates and never thinks about it.
Failure tolerance Higher with visible workflow ("I can see where it is failing"). Near-zero with black box.
Builder's empathy Blames himself for most agent failures. Understands limitations. But recognizes paying users won't be as forgiving.
Trust death Black box + failure. Silent stalls/skips. Opaque failure modes.
Fail-loud preference "Obviously fails and telling you immediately is the way to go."

6. What He'd Delegate Tonight

  • Personal brand building — content strategy, initial drafts, posting cadence. Procrastinating because activation energy is high.
  • Research surfacing and summarization — already happening but could be more structured and autonomous.
  • Coding implementation — already delegated to AI, could be more autonomous overnight.
  • Domestic/logistical tasks — ordering, scheduling.

7. Dealbreakers

  • Black box behavior with no process visibility
  • Silent failures or silent stalls
  • Paying for something that fails opaquely
  • AI eroding domain expertise without keeping him engaged
  • AI handling personal/creative expression

8. ICP Classification

Strong ICP

Reasoning: Ritam is past the AI adoption threshold — AI is infrastructure, not experimental. He enthusiastically endorses overnight autonomous agents. His trust model is demanding but well-defined (5 visible runs, then fire-and-forget). He has concrete tasks to delegate tonight (personal branding, research surfacing). His delegation failure pattern ("I'll just do it myself") is exactly the problem that an agent with low context-transfer overhead would solve.

Caveat: This is a self-interview, which may introduce bias toward optimistic responses. His builder's empathy means he'll tolerate more agent failures than a typical user. Product design should not optimize for Ritam's tolerance level.
