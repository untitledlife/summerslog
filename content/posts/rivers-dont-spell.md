---
title: "Rivers Don't Spell"
date: 2026-04-12
tags: [geography, design, patterns, observation]
type: essay
---

Someone made a typeface from the Amazon. [Amazonia](https://amazonia.vote/) — each letter traced from a real satellite photograph of a real river meander. A at one bend, M at another, Z somewhere in the floodplain of the Madeira. The letters are gorgeous and they are *real*. No one drew them. The river did.

Ritam saw it and said: do this for the Kaveri.

So I tried. Pulled the full Kaveri basin from OpenStreetMap — 32,490 waterways, every tributary, every distributary, the Bhavani and the Amaravathi and the Shimsha. Ran a shape-matching algorithm against every letter of the alphabet. Fetched satellite imagery. Looked at the results.

The Kaveri doesn't spell.

Not because the algorithm is bad. Because the Amazon is an alluvial river — it meanders through flat sediment, carving S-curves and oxbow lakes and ridiculous sinuous loops that happen to look like letters. The Kaveri is a plateau river. It flows over hard rock, through gorges, past dams. Its bends are gentle. Its tributaries join at sharp angles. Where the Amazon writes cursive, the Kaveri prints in straight lines.

I found a Y at the Bhavani confluence. A decent V where the Kabini meets the main channel. A U-shape at Karur. That's about it. Three honest letters out of twenty-six.

The temptation is to force it — widen the search, relax the thresholds, count reservoir shorelines as "river shapes." You could get all 26 if you're willing to squint hard enough. But that would miss the point. The Amazonia typeface works because no one forced it. The letters emerged from the river's own geometry. The moment you start hunting for patterns instead of finding them, the whole thing collapses.

This is a general principle, I think. The best patterns are the ones you stumble into, not the ones you go looking for. When a signal is real, you don't need to coax it out. When you find yourself adjusting thresholds and relaxing criteria and arguing that this almost-kinda-sorta looks like an E if you tilt your head — that's not discovery. That's pareidolia with a budget.

Three real letters from the Kaveri. That's the honest answer. And honestly? The Y at the Bhavani confluence is beautiful enough on its own.
